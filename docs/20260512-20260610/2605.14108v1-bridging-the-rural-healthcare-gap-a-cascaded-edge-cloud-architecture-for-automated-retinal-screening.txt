# Bridging the Rural Healthcare Gap: A Cascaded Edge-Cloud Architecture for Automated Retinal Screening 

Nishi Doshi 

University of Southern California Los Angeles, USA 

nishimit@usc.edu 

Shrey Shah 

University of Southern California Los Angeles, USA 

shrey@alumni.usc.edu 

Abstract —Diabetic Retinopathy (DR) is one of the leading causes of preventable blindness, yet rural regions often lack the specialists and infrastructure needed for early detection. Although cloud-based deep learning systems offer high accuracy, they face significant challenges in these settings due to high latency, limited bandwidth, and high data transmission costs. To address these challenges, we propose a two-tier edge-cloud cascade on the public APTOS 2019 Blindness Detection dataset. Tier 1 runs a lightweight MobileNetV3-small model on a local clinic device to perform a binary triage between Referable DR (Classes 2–4) and Non-referable DR (Classes 0–1). Tier 2 runs a RETFound-DINOv2 model in the cloud for ordinal severity grading, but only on the subset of images flagged as referable by Tier 1. On a stratified APTOS test split of 733 images, Tier 1

reaches 98 .99% sensitivity and 84 .37% specificity at a validation-tuned high-sensitivity threshold. The default cascade forwards 

49 .52% of test images to Tier 2, reducing cloud calls by 50 .48% 

relative to using a cloud-based model for all images. In the deployed 4-class output space (Class 0–1 / Class 2 / Class 3 / Class 

4), the cascade obtains 80 .49% accuracy and 0.8167 quadratic weighted kappa; the cloud-only baseline obtains 80 .76% accuracy and 0.8184 quadratic weighted kappa. On APTOS, the cascade cuts cloud use by about half with a modest drop in grading performance. 

Index Terms —Diabetic Retinopathy, Edge-Cloud Cascade, MobileNetV3-small, RETFound-DINOv2, Retinal Screening, tele-ophthalmology 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Diabetic Retinopathy (DR) is a microvascular complication of diabetes mellitus and a leading cause of preventable blind-ness in working-age adults. A 2021 meta-analysis estimates DR prevalence at 22 .27% of diabetic adults and projects 161 

million people with DR by 2045 [1] [2]; the Global Burden of Disease 2020 study attributes 3.3 million blindness cases and 10 .8 million moderate-to-severe vision impairment cases to DR-related causes [3]. DR is graded on the 5-stage Inter-national Clinical Diabetic Retinopathy (ICDR) scale (Figure 1); Classes 0–1 are treated as non-referable and Classes 2–4

as referable [2]. Manual grading is labor-intensive and requires ophthalmo-logic training that is often unavailable in rural clinics. With a projected 18 million health-worker shortage by 2030 in lower-income regions [4], automated triage can help extend screening capacity. One approach is to use a large cloud model, which works well in connected hospitals but is harder to use in low-connectivity settings. Cloud-only DR screening faces three practical barriers in rural settings. 1) High-resolution fundus images can be large ( 5–20 MB), so uploads over low-bandwidth links can delay screening workflows. 2) In regions where data is metered or expensive, repeated image uploads can add meaningful operating cost for small clinics; cost remains a known barrier to digital health adoption in low- and middle-income settings [5]. 3) Cloud models depend on reliable connectivity. In the Google-Thailand DR deployment, internet connectivity problems prevented the system from returning results in several clinics [6]. Small edge models avoid the network dependency but are weaker for detailed grading. We therefore evaluate a two-tier cascade: Tier 1 performs local referable/non-referable triage, and Tier 2 performs ordinal severity grading only for images forwarded by Tier 1. The final output is reported in 4 deployed classes. The cascade is motivated by four asymmetries: 1) Task difficulty: Referable/non-referable triage is acoarser task than severity grading, so it can be handled by a lightweight local model. 2) Operational requirements: Tier 1 runs locally without a network call, while Tier 2 uses a larger cloud model and requires image upload. 3) Population distribution and cloud calls: In Asia Pa-cific Tele-ophthalmology Society (APTOS) 2019 , 59 .4% 

of images are non-referable, giving an upper bound on possible image-count savings. 4) Cost of error: A Tier-1 false negative prevents a refer-able case from reaching the cloud model, while a false positive only adds one cloud inference. We therefore tune Tier 1 toward high sensitivity. This paper investigates the following research questions, focusing on resource limitations in rural screening workflows:

II. R ELATED WORKS 

Automated DR analysis progressed from early CNN-based systems to deeper CNN architectures such as ResNet and multi-channel CNNs [7], [8]. We briefly discuss related work on cloud-based, edge-based, and collaborative edge–cloud models. The FDA-cleared IDx-DR system provides a clinical refer-ence point, with reported sensitivity of 95% and specificity of 

91% [9]. Recent high-capacity models also use transformer-based designs; Dual-SwinOrd reports 87 .98% accuracy and Quadratic weighted kappa of 0.937 [10]. Edge models target local inference under limited compute and connectivity. Convolutional Vision Transformer (CvT) combines local feature extraction of CNNs with the global context of transformers and achieves 92 .5% accuracy, 91 .20% 

sensitivity and 94 .0% specificity [11]. Prior edge-cloud DR frameworks preprocess fundus im-ages locally before upload, reporting up to 80% lower data transmission [12]. Our cascade instead uses the edge model for referable/non-referable triage and forwards only referable images for cloud grading. III. D ATASET

We propose and evaluate a two-tier edge-cloud cascade. Tier 1 runs locally on a smaller model, MobileNetV3-small, and performs binary triage: Referable DR (ICDR Classes 2–4)versus Non-referable DR (Classes 0–1). Images predicted as non-referable stop at Tier 1 and are assigned to the deployed Non-referable class (Class 0–1) output. Images predicted as referable are forwarded to Tier 2, where a larger RETFound-DINOv2 model assigns the final severity grade. Figure 2 shows the End-to-end (E2E) cascaded pipeline with Tier 1 and Tier 

2 stages. As described in Section III, the images in the dataset are quite large and vary in size. Thus, preprocessing becomes a major step of the pipeline. The two tiers use different preprocessing pipelines because they use different pretrained models. Tier 1 follows the preprocessing expected by the released MobileNetV3-small checkpoint by ClementP, while Tier 2 follows the preprocessing used for the RETFound-DINOv2 ordinal model. 

A. Preprocessing 

APTOS images vary substantially in size and contain dark borders around the circular retinal field of view, as shown in Figure 1. We therefore apply deterministic preprocessing before inference. The exact preprocessing differs for each tier. 1) Tier 1 preprocessing: Tier 1 uses the native ClementP MobileNetV3 preprocessing pipeline associated with the 

FundusDRGrading-mobilenetv3_small_100 

checkpoint. This pipeline has three steps: a) Autocropping: The image is cropped to the retinal field of view using the red channel. Pixels above an intensity threshold of 10 define the foreground mask, and the bounding box of this mask is used to remove the dark border around the fundus. This focuses the input on the retinal fundus and removes most non-informative black background. b) Aspect-preserving resize: The cropped image is resized so that its longest side matches the target input size while preserving the original aspect ratio. This avoids stretching the retina into a square. c) Padding and normalization: The resized image is zero-padded to 512 × 512 and normalized us-ing ImageNet mean and standard deviation. This matches the input distribution expected by the model checkpoint. 2) Tier 2 preprocessing: Tier 2 uses the preprocessing pipeline for the RETFound-DINOv2 ordinal grading model. a) Fundus cropping: A bounding-box crop removes the dark border around the circular retinal field of view. The crop is computed from the non-background retinal pixels, so the model focuses on the fundus rather than the surrounding black region. b) Resizing: The cropped image is resized to 392 ×

392 using bilinear interpolation. c) Normalization: The resized image is converted to a tensor and normalized using ImageNet mean and standard deviation, matching the expected input normalization of the pretrained backbone. For validation and test images, this preprocessing is deter-ministic. Training images may additionally use augmentation, but no random augmentation is applied when reporting Tier 2

or cascade metrics.

B. Tier 1: Edge triage model 

Tier 1 uses MobileNetV3-small as the local triage model. Rather than starting from standard ImageNet weights, we use the released ClementP 

FundusDRGrading-mobilenetv3_small_100 

checkpoint [14]. This checkpoint was trained with 512 × 512 

fundus images and uses a one-logit ordinal output head. For our cascade, the ordinal output is passed through a sigmoid to produce a referable score, where higher values indicate stronger evidence for ICDR Classes 2–4. The decision threshold is selected on the APTOS validation split using the validation-set sensitivity-specificity curve to prioritize sensitiv-ity; the held-out test split is not consulted at any point during threshold selection. The selected operating point is 

σ(z) ≥ 0.6977 , (1) where z is the model logit and σ(·) is the sigmoid function. We choose the threshold to keep sensitivity high, because missed referable cases are the most important Tier 1 error. This sensitivity-biased threshold is used because Tier 1

controls whether an image reaches the cloud model. A false negative at this stage means that a referable case is not forwarded for severity grading. A false positive is less harmful in this pipeline: it triggers an extra cloud inference but does not suppress a potentially referable case. Operationally, Tier 1 reduces the number of images sent to the cloud. Images predicted as non-referable are assigned to the deployed Class 0–1 output and stop locally. Images predicted as referable are forwarded to Tier 2 for severity grading. 

C. Tier 2: Cloud severity model 

Tier 2 uses RETFound-DINOv2 Vision Transformer Large with patch size 14 (ViT-L/14) as the cloud-side severity model. It is a large retinal foundation model based on the RETFound framework [15], with the DINOv2 [16] self-supervised objec-tive applied to color fundus photographs, making it a suitable initialization for DR grading. We do not claim this as a new model contribution; in this work, it is used as a pretrained backbone for the cloud stage of the cascade. For APTOS, we attach a one-logit ordinal regression head and partially fine-tune the model on the training split. The model is trained using mean squared error (MSE) against the integer ICDR grade, so the output is a continuous severity score rather than a softmax probability over classes. At infer-ence time, the score is rounded and clipped to the valid ICDR range {0, 1, 2, 3, 4}.For standalone Tier 2 reporting, we evaluate the model using the full 5-class APTOS metrics. For comparison with the deployed cascade, Classes 0 and 1 are collapsed into a single non-referable class, giving the shared 4-class output space: Class 0-1, Class 2, Class 3, and Class 4.

D. Evaluation metrics 

We report metrics separately for Tier 1, Tier 2, and the end-to-end cascade. Tier 1 is evaluated as a binary referable/non-referable triage model. Tier 2 is evaluated as an ordinal DR grading model. The cascade is evaluated in the deployed four-class output space: Class 0–1, Class 2, Class 3, and Class 4.For Tier 1, sensitivity is the primary safety metric because a false negative means that a referable DR case is not forwarded to the cloud model. We therefore tune the Tier 1 threshold on the validation set to prioritize sensitivity.  

> •

Confusion Matrix : It compares the model’s predicted labels with the true labels, showing how many samples were correctly and incorrectly assigned to each class. • Sensitivity : It measures the fraction of actual referable cases that are correctly identified. This is the main Tier 

1 metric because missed referable cases are the most important error in the first stage of the cascade.  

> •

Specificity : It measures the fraction of non-referable cases that are correctly stopped locally. Higher specificity reduces unnecessary cloud calls, but in this pipeline we prioritize sensitivity over specificity.  

> •

Accuracy : Accuracy measures the fraction of correctly classified samples. For the cascade, accuracy is reported in the deployed four-class output space.  

> •

Quadratic Weighted Kappa score (κ): Quadratic weighted kappa (QWK), denoted by κ, measures agree-ment between predicted and true ordinal labels while penalizing mistakes according to their distance. This is useful for DR grading because predicting Class 4 as Class 

0 is more severe than predicting Class 4 as Class 3.We report κ for Tier 2 and for the end-to-end cascade because both produce ordinal DR grades. The higher value of QWK indicates substantial agreement between predictions and ordinal ground truth ( κ ≥ 0.8 typically interpreted as substantial agreement).  

> •

Cloud-call rate ( X%) and image-count saving ( Y %):The cloud-call rate measures the fraction of test images forwarded from Tier 1 to Tier 2. The image-count saving 

Y is the complement: Y = 100 − X. This metric measures the reduction in the number of cloud calls. It is not a byte-level bandwidth measurement, since actual bandwidth depends on image resolution, compression, and transmission format. V. E XPERIMENT SETUP 

We split the public APTOS 2019 labeled dataset into train, validation, and test sets using a fixed random seed of 42 . The test set contains 20% of the full dataset. From the remaining 

80% , we use 80% for training and 20% for validation. This gives a final split of 64% train, 16% validation, and 20% test. The split is stratified by ICDR class so that each class is represented in the train, validation, and test sets. We use the same split for Tier 1, Tier 2, and the end-to-end cascade evaluation. This ensures that the cloud-only and cascade results are compared on the same held-out test images. The experiments are organized around the two stages of the cascade and the final end-to-end pipeline. 

A. Tier 1: edge triage model 

Tier 1 is evaluated as a binary referable/non-referable triage model. ICDR Classes 0 and 1 are mapped to the non-referable class, while Classes 2, 3, and 4 are mapped to the referable class. We use the MobileNetV3-small backbone for Tier 1.The model outputs a single ordinal logit, which is converted to a referable score using the sigmoid function. The decision threshold is selected on the validation set to target high sensitivity. After fixing this threshold, we evaluate Tier 1 on the held-out APTOS test split. We report the binary confusion matrix, accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity. The primary metric for this tier is sensitivity, because the local model is used as the gatekeeper for cloud referral. Tier 1 inference is lightweight enough for local deployment, but this study does not include a formal edge-device bench-mark. 

B. Tier 2: cloud severity model 

Tier 2 is trained for 5-class ordinal DR grading on the APTOS split. We use RETFound-DINOv2 ViT-L/14 as the pretrained cloud-side backbone. The checkpoint provides the pretrained encoder, so we attach a freshly initialized single-logit linear head of dimension 1024 → 1.The model output is treated as a continuous severity score. During training, we optimize MSE between this score and the integer ICDR grade in {0, 1, 2, 3, 4}. At inference time, the output is rounded and clipped to the valid ICDR range. This ordinal-regression setup penalizes large grading mistakes more than small ones, which matches the ordinal nature of DR severity and is consistent with reporting QWK ( κ). We partially fine-tune the model instead of updating all parameters. The last two transformer blocks, the post-pool LayerNorm layer ( fc_norm ), and the new one-logit head are trainable. The earlier transformer blocks, patch-embedding layer, and positional embeddings remain frozen. This gives approximately 25 .2M trainable parameters for the APTOS run. Optimization uses AdamW for 25 epochs with 16 bit floating point mixed precision, weight decay 0.01 , a backbone learning rate of 1×10 −5, and a head learning rate of 1×10 −3.The best checkpoint is selected using validation-set 5-class QWK ( κ). For standalone Tier 2 reporting, we first evaluate the model in the full 5-class ICDR output space. To compare this cloud-only baseline with the deployed cascade, we also collapse Classes 0 and 1 into a single non-referable class and recompute the confusion matrix in the shared 4-class output space: Class 

0–1, Class 2, Class 3, and Class 4.

C. End-to-end cascaded pipeline 

After evaluating the two tiers separately, we evaluate the full cascade on the held-out APTOS test split. Tier 1 first assigns each image a referable score. Images below the validation-selected threshold are stopped locally and assigned to the deployed non-referable output class (Class 0–1). Images above the threshold are forwarded to Tier 2, where RETFound-DINOv2 assigns the final severity grade. The cascade is evaluated in the same 4-class output space used for the cloud-only comparison: Class 0–1, Class 2, Class 

3, and Class 4. We report the cascaded confusion matrix, accuracy, QWK( κ), and the cloud-call rate. The main comparison is against the cloud-only Tier 2 base-line, where every test image is processed by the RETFound-DINOv2 model. This comparison measures the trade-off in-troduced by the cascade: how much cloud use is reduced, and how much performance is retained in the deployed 4-class output space. C. Cascaded pipeline results 

Figure 3d shows the confusion matrix for the end-to-end cascaded pipeline on the held-out APTOS test split. In this setup, Tier 1 acts as the forwarding gate: images with a referable score above the validation-selected threshold are sent to Tier 2, while images below the threshold stop locally as non-referable. For forwarded images, Tier 2 produces the final severity grade. Unlike a safety-floor variant, this cascade allows Tier 2

to override the Tier 1 referral decision and assign a forwarded image back to the non-referable Class 0–1 group. This is useful when Tier 1 produces a false-positive referral, because the cloud model can correct the final deployed label. The cascade is evaluated in the deployed 4-class output space: Class 0–1, Class 2, Class 3, and Class 4. The accuracy is 80 .49% .The corresponding 4-class QWK is κ = 0 .8167 .Table III compares the cascaded pipeline with the cloud-only Tier 2 baseline. The cloud-only baseline sends every test image to Tier 2, giving a cloud-call rate of 100% . The cascade forwards 49 .52% of test images and avoids cloud calls for the remaining 50 .48% .The cascade achieves nearly the same 4-class performance as the cloud-only baseline while reducing cloud calls by about half. The accuracy decreases from 80 .76% to 80 .49% ,and QWK decreases from 0.8184 to 0.8167 . This suggests that, under the APTOS split used in this study, the cascade can retain most of the cloud-only grading performance while avoiding unnecessary cloud inference for non-referable cases. VII. C ONCLUSION 

This paper evaluated a two-tier edge-cloud cascade for automated DR screening on the public APTOS 2019 dataset.
