Abstract (248) 35 

Purpose: To investigate the correlation of retinoblastoma (RB) aqueous humor (AH)–derived 36 

extracellular vesicle (EV) surface markers, particularly CD133, with clinical features of RB. 37 

Methods: AH samples (n=22) collected at diagnosis (n=17) or primary enucleation (n=5) from RB eyes 38 

were profiled using magnetic bead-based flow cytometry. Median fluorescence intensities (MFI) of 39 

tetraspanins (CD9, CD63, CD81) and CD133 were quantified and correlated with International 40 

Intraocular RB Classification (IIRC) stage, seeding location, seeding morphology, and enucleation. 41 

Results: CD133 + EVs were detected in 16 of 22 (73%) samples. While CD133 levels showed a non-42 

significant increasing trend with advanced IIRC stages, CD133 expression was significantly elevated in 43 

eyes with vitreous seeding vs. subretinal only (P = 0.010). Cuzick’s trend analysis revealed a significant 44 

association between rising CD133 % MFI and complex seeding (P < 0.001). Conversely, CD63 displayed 45 

a significant inverse trend, with decreasing % MFI associated with complex seeding (P < 0.001). 46 

CD133 high eyes (MFI>10,000, n=9) exhibited increased enucleation (P = 0.007) and predominantly 47 

sphere/cloud seeding patterns (P = 0.001). CD133 high eyes demonstrated a coordinated CD63/CD81 co-48 

expression pattern (r = 0.80), absent in the CD133 low group. 49 

Conclusions: Elevated CD133 level and percentage were associated with vitreous involvement and 50 

complex seeding morphology, suggesting potential as a minimally invasive indicator of disease burden. 51 

The inverse relationship between CD63 and seeding severity, combined with CD63/CD81 remodeling in 52 

CD133 high eyes, suggests an EV profile indicative of aggressive disease. These findings support the 53 

association of CD133 + EVs with advanced RB seeding and warrant further prospective validation. 54

Introduction 60 

Retinoblastoma (RB) is the most common pediatric intraocular malignancy 1 accounting for about 61 

3% of all childhood cancers 2. Around 300 cases in the United States and 8,000 global cases are 62 

diagnosed every year 2,3 . The goal of treatment is to save the child's life and if possible, save the 63 

eye with some degree of vision. Traditional tumor biopsy in RB is contraindicated due to the 64 

risks of extraocular spread of the cancer 4-9 ; thus, tumor tissue is available only at enucleation. 65 

This limits understanding of the disease at both the genotypic and phenotypic level 4-9 and hinders 66 

application of genomics to treatment decision making due to lack of in vivo RB markers from 67 

eyes undergoing therapy 10 . These challenges highlight the clinical need for innovative 68 

methodologies. 69 

A significant breakthrough in 2017 demonstrated that aqueous humor (AH), an ocular fluid in 70 

direct contact with intraocular tumors, could serve as a source of tumor-derived cell-free DNA 71 

(cfDNA), enabling safe, minimally invasive liquid biopsy in RB. However, limitations to 72 

analysis due to factors such as decreasing DNA concentrations with ongoing treatment 11,12 

> 73

highlight the need for complementary biomarkers that reflect tumor biology through mechanisms 74 

beyond genomic alterations. 75 

Beyond cfDNA, extracellular vesicles (EVs) have emerged as important mediators of 76 

intercellular communication and hold promise as non-genomic biomarkers 13,14 . EVs are 77 

heterogeneous nanosized lipid membrane-enclosed vesicles secreted by all kinds of cells into 78 

biological fluids, carrying proteins, nucleic acids, and lipids. EVs are highly stable due to their 79 

protection by a lipid bilayer. They reflect the status of the cell, rendering them suitable as a 80 

biomarker 15 , with presence of integral membrane protein markers like tetraspanins (e.g., CD9, 81

CD63, and CD81) serving as common EV markers due to their functional role and extensive 82 

presence. These vesicles are abundant and found in various ocular fluids, including the AH 16,17 ,83 

making them accessible through minimally invasive sampling. 84 

Our previous work established that AH in a subset of RB eyes contains a distinct population of 85 

CD133-enriched EVs, a known cancer stem cell marker 18 , not present in glaucoma or uveal 86 

melanoma samples, supporting their tumor specificity 19 . This was validated using multiple EV 87 

characterization platforms, including MACSPlex, Single-particle interferometric reflectance 88 

imaging sensor (SP-IRIS), and Single Extracellular VEsicle Nanoscopy (SEVEN) assays 19 .89 

MACSPlex is a multidimensional bead-based assay with 39 antigens and two controls tied to 90 

APC conjugated CD9, CD63, and CD81 antibodies, allowing extensive investigation of known 91 

cancer and cell type markers. We found that CD133 is frequently co-localized with CD63 and 92 

CD81, supporting a unique RB-derived EV signature 17 . While tetraspanins serve as useful pan-93 

EV surface markers, they may not provide the precision needed to discern RB-derived EVs (RB-94 

EVs) from other diseases or differentiate disease subtypes within RB. Thus, tumor-specific 95 

surface markers such as CD133 may offer enhanced specificity in profiling RB disease state. 96 

However, CD133 expression across RB samples was heterogeneous, and one subset of cases 97 

demonstrated little to no detectable CD133. This variability raised the question of whether 98 

CD133 + EVs reflect clinically meaningful features of disease behavior. 99 

Tumor seeding, defined as the spread of RB cells into adjacent liquid or semi-liquid spaces, is a 100 

key determinant of prognosis and treatment complexity 20 . This seeding has been described as 101 

both vitreous and subretinal based on the anatomical location into either the vitreous chamber or 102 

behind the retina. Seeding also can be classified by breadth of involvement, using a quadrant 103 

system of 0-4 to describe involvement. Seeding plays a central role in RB staging, with its 104

from systemic circulation, limiting chemotherapy efficacy 22 . Both vitreous and subretinal 107 

seeding are major contributors to treatment failure and enucleation, even with advances in 108 

localized therapies 23-25 . Given the biological importance of seeding morphology and extent, an 109 

AH-based biomarker capable of reflecting these features could provide clinically actionable 110 

information. 111 

Building on our initial observations, we hypothesized that CD133 signal intensity on AH-derived 112 

EVs correlates with clinically relevant features of RB including seeding location, morphology, 113 

and enucleation. In this study, we analyzed a diagnostic cohort using multiplexed bead-based EV 114 

profiling (MACSPlex) to determine whether CD133 + EVs correlate with complex tumor seeding .115

Results 175 

Demographic and clinical features of the diagnostic cohort of RB patients 176 

To correlate clinical and molecular data, we compiled demographic and disease-specific features 177 

from each eye. These features included RB1 germline mutation status, laterality, age at diagnosis, 178 

sex, tumor growth pattern, presence of subretinal or vitreous seeding, and enucleation status. AH 179 

samples were grouped by IIRC stage at diagnosis and further stratified by seeding extent, defined 180 

as the number of quadrants involved (0–4; Figure 1A). Each AH sample was analyzed 181 

independently. Two patients contributed bilateral samples, which were treated as separate data 182 

points for EV and clinical comparisons (Figure 1A). Using MACSPlex analysis, a heatmap of the 183 

22 samples demonstrated robust detection across all antigens, with notable co-dominance of 184 

CD63 and CD81. CD133 expression exhibited substantial variability, with 6 samples negative for 185 

CD133 and 16 samples positive for CD133, showing varying signal intensities (Figure 1B). A 186 

representative MACSPlex profile is shown in Figure 1C. 187

Discussion 266 

In this study, we investigated the association between CD133 expression on AH-derived EVs and 267 

key clinical features of RB. Analysis of a diagnostic AH cohort revealed substantial 268 

heterogeneity in CD133 signal intensity, ranging from undetectable to markedly elevated levels 269 

in advanced RB seeding phenotype. CD133 is a well-recognized biomarker for tumor-derived 270 

EVs in several malignancies, with extensive evidence in colorectal cancer linking CD133  EVs 271 

to modulation of the tumor microenvironment, promotion of tumor progression, and poorer 272 

clinical outcomes 27–30 .273 

Previous in vitro studies have highlighted the biological significance of CD133 in RB cell lines 31 ,274 

particularly its associations with stemness, proliferation, differentiation, and chemoresistance of 275 

cancer stem cells (CSC). However, the presence of CD133 on AH-derived EVs in RB and its 276 

clinical relevance have remained unclear. Our findings address this knowledge gap by correlating 277 

CD133  EV profiles with clinically relevant RB phenotypes, including seeding pattern and 278 

morphology, along with enucleation status. 279

Our previous work 19 identified robust CD133 positivity in multiple RB AH-derived EV samples 280 

and RB cell lines, prompting further investigation into its origin and significance. We 281 

demonstrated that CD133 expression was consistently associated with CD63 and CD81 co-282 

expression, a finding aligned with our prior ExoView-based analyses that established 283 

CD63/CD81 co-dominance as a marker of tumor-derived EVs in RB 17 . In the present study, this 284 

pattern was supported by observation that CD133 high samples demonstrated a strong CD63/CD81 285 

co-expression signature (Figure 5C), consistent with a coordinated EV phenotype. 286 

Notably, higher CD133 trended with more advanced IIRC stage and a greater likelihood of 287 

primary enucleation (Figure 5B), suggesting that CD133 elevation may accompany more 288 

clinically severe disease, though not functioning as an outcome predictor in this dataset. 289 

When we looked more closely at tumor seeding characteristics, we observed differences in 290 

CD133 signal depending on the location of seeding. Eyes with vitreous seeds had significantly 291 

higher CD133 expression than those with subretinal seeding (Figure 3D). This may reflect 292 

differences in EV trafficking within the eye. The subretinal space is anatomically isolated and 293 

protected by the blood-retinal barrier, which may restrict the release or movement of EVs. In 294 

contrast, the vitreous cavity is more open and better connected to the anterior chamber, making it 295 

more likely that EVs from this region reach the AH, where we collected our samples. 296 

We also saw that CD133 high eyes tended to have more sphere and cloud seeding (Figure 5B). 297 

Previous studies have demonstrated that there are higher local treatment failure risks with diffuse 298 

seeding in comparison to focal seeding 32 . Vitreous seeding is delineated into 3 distinct patterns 299 

based on the spread of tumor cells: dust, sphere, and cloud. Dust seeding is described as the 300 

result of cellular infiltration and consists of individual tumor cells, and cloud seeding is a result 301

The ability to detect aggressive seeding patterns through a minimally invasive AH tap could alter 331 

clinical decision making. Eyes with high CD133  EV levels might warrant more intensive local 332 

therapy, earlier intravitreal chemotherapy, or closer follow-up intervals, even if traditional 333 

imaging findings are equivocal. Such an approach could help preempt treatment failure and 334 

reduce enucleation rates. However, prospective validation, ideally with longitudinal sampling, is 335 

required before any clinical application. 336 

This study has several limitations, including a modest, single-institution sample size and the 337 

absence of direct validation of EV origin. While CD133  EVs were interpreted as tumor-338 

associated based on co-expression with canonical markers and clinical correlations, further 339 

functional studies are needed to confirm this. The use of a median-based CD133 cutoff was 340 

exploratory and required refinement in larger cohorts. Additionally, anatomical differences in EV 341 

access to the AH may bias detectability toward vitreous rather than subretinal disease. Variability 342 

in EV abundance across clinical samples may also influence MFI values, although percentage 343 

normalization partially mitigates this. 344 

Despite these limitations, our findings provide novel evidence linking AH-derived EVs to 345 

clinically relevant tumor seeding features and enucleation status in RB. These findings, along 346
