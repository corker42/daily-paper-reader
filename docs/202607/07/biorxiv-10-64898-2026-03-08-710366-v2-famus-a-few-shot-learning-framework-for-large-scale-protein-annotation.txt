Abstract 6

Predicting gene function is a pivotal and challenging step in genomic and metagenomic data 7

analysis. Current automatic annotation tools typically rely on the single most similar sequence 8

from the query database and struggle to robustly set hit thresholds for annotation. The sparsity 9

of proteins per annotation makes it challenging to confidently assign gene function for 10 

underrepresented families. Here, we present a contrastive learning framework for functional 11 

annotation. FAMUS (Functional Annotation Method Using Supervised contrastive learning) 12 

compares query sequences to a full array of profile Hidden Markov Models and transforms the 13 

similarity scores into a condensed vector space that minimizes the distance of proteins from 14 

the same family. The similarity scores of a query to all profiles are used for its representation 15 

instead of considering only the top-ranking hit. Unannotated sequences are incorporated as 16 

negative examples during training, enabling robust detection of proteins that fall outside the 17 

scope of the reference database without requiring a user-defined threshold. Using this approach, 18 

FAMUS outperformed KEGG’s native KofamScan for KEGG Orthology annotation and 19 

InterPro’s InterProScan for PANTHER family annotation. We thus created four protein 20 

annotation models using protein families from the KEGG Orthology, InterPro family, 21 

OrthoDB, and EggNOG databases. All four models are available as a conda package and via 22 

our user-friendly web server, allowing users to annotate large-scale datasets. FAMUS is the 23 

first comprehensive and modular annotation framework based on contrastive learning. It 24 

supports both pre-defined and user-specific databases for tailored annotation, and can be easily 25 

integrated into any genomic and metagenomic analysis pipeline to facilitate accurate, large-26 

scale functional annotation. 27

Introduction 32 

Advances in sequencing technologies and bioinformatic approaches over the past decades have 33 

greatly increased the rate at which we can sequence and assemble genomes and metagenomes. 34 

A key informative analysis following the assembly is gene functional annotation, where each 35 

putative gene is assigned a function based on an annotated database. The most widely used 36 

tools for gene annotation are based on sequence similarity, from simple BLAST 1 searches to 37 

deep learning and state-of-the-art language models 2–6. One of the sensitive and efficient 38 

approaches to functional annotation involves using profile Hidden Markov Models (pHMMs), 39 

statistical models that capture position-specific patterns in sequence families. Since these 40 

models represent families composed of multiple sequences, they can capture complex and 41 

subtle sequence patterns, eliminate the need for pairwise sequence alignment, and reduce the 42 

total number of comparisons required to make a prediction compared to sequence-sequence 43 

search approaches 7,8 . Gene sequences are compared to pHMMs to acquire measures of 44 

similarity to the protein family represented by the profile. 45 

Although straightforward and effective, three major caveats to this approach limit its accuracy. 46 

First, annotation is typically based on the best score (“winner takes it all”), and this strategy 47 

does not make full use of all the bit score information generated in the search process against 48 

all the families in the database. In most cases, using the highest similarity scores is adequate, 49 

but accounting for entire similarity patterns may allow better annotation of distant homologs 50 

and ambiguous cases. Second, for both orthology- and function-based pHMMs, performance 51 

is affected by the criteria used to cluster proteins into families. E.g., pHMMs are prone to the 52 

inclusion of genes with low intra-cluster similarity. This could lead to low-information pHMMs 53 

with low specificity, yielding high rates of false positives or low rates of high-scoring hits. 54 

Third, defining a reliable annotation threshold is non-trivial, as optimal cutoffs vary across 55 

protein families due to differences in evolutionary rates, sequence diversity, and family size, 56 

often requiring manual curation or family-specific tuning. 57 

These challenges are well exemplified by the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes 58 

(KEGG) 9–11 , a comprehensive knowledge base for systematic analysis of gene functions, 59 

linking genetic data with higher-order functional information through standardized pathway 60 

maps and molecular networks. KEGG Orthology is a database of functional gene orthologs, 61 

also termed KOs. In KEGG, human curation is a necessary part of the KO assignment process, 62 

since this assignment is often context-dependent, and expert biological insight might contradict 63 

the relationships learned by automated annotation tools. The result is a significant number of 64 

KOs with highly diverse sequences in terms of similarity, which are difficult for automation 65 

tools to generalize. For example, despite their distinct functions, the KO labeled K13194 66 

includes sequences of both ADARB1, a double-stranded RNA editing enzyme, and ADARB2, 67 

a catalytically inactive homolog that is believed to be a competitive inhibitor of ADARB1 12 .68 

Such cases are expected to lead to pHMMs with low specificity, as described above. Applying 69 

more advanced classification methodologies is also problematic, as KEGG contains more than 70 

24,000 families, making the training of accurate multi-class classifiers challenging. Classifying 71 

sequences to a KEGG protein family directly would require either a very complex model or 72

families with highly variable sequence diversity and sparse representation. 79 

Similar challenges were encountered in other fields, such as computer vision, where a common 80 

task is to annotate facial images with the identity of the person given a single or very few 81 

images. Contrastive learning has emerged as an elegant solution to facial image annotation, 82 

comprising methods such as Siamese Neural Networks (SNNs) 16 and later triplet networks 17 .83 

Contrastive learning models can learn effectively even with very limited training data by 84 

transforming the task of classification to a task of comparison. In the training process, a single 85 

neural network is used to generate a low-dimensional representation of different objects and is 86 

optimized such that objects with the same label are co-located in the embedding space. This 87 

dramatically increases the number of possible samples for training due to the combinatorial 88 

nature of sampling multiple objects and decreases the dimensionality of the output space, 89 

alleviating the need for extensive models. Lifted Structured Feature Embedding 18 extended this 90 

paradigm by leveraging all positive and negative pairs within a batch rather than individual 91 

triplets, yielding a more stable and informative training signal. Later methods, such as 92 

Supervised Contrastive learning (SupCon) 19 replaced the margin-based loss with a93 

temperature-scaled, normalized softmax, giving finer control over how much each pair 94 

contributes according to its difficulty. Previous approaches that utilised contrastive learning for 95 

biological sequence analysis include SENSE 20 , a tool that encodes k-mer presence and position 96 

for alignment-free sequence embedding and pairwise distance estimation; CLEAN 6, a tool that 97 

encodes transformer model outputs for enzyme classification using both SupCon and triplet 98 

networks; and ATGO 3, a triplet-network-based model for Gene Ontology (GO) annotation. 99 

CLEAN and ATGO both use representations derived from language models as their contrastive 100 

model’s input. While useful, the preprocessing step of using language models in CLEAN and 101 

ATGO represents a significant computational overhead that limits the scale at which they can 102 

be used to annotate a large number of proteins. In addition, they were both developed and 103 

evaluated on one type of annotation (EC numbers 21 and Gene Ontology (GO) 22 , respectively), 104 

and were not designed to support user-defined custom databases or to identify sequences that 105 

fall outside the scope of the reference database. Thus, a general and scalable approach is needed 106 

to advance our understanding of environmental microbes’ functional potential. 107 

We adopted a SupCon-based approach to address the challenges of full genome/metagenome 108 

functional annotation, which we call “Functional Annotation Method Using Supervised 109 

contrastive learning” (FAMUS). FAMUS explicitly classifies sequences to a specific protein 110 

family using a neural network trained with SupCon, or identifies them as out-of-scope when 111 

they are not sufficiently similar to any family in the reference database. This is achieved by 112 

incorporating unannotated protein sequences as negative examples during training, enabling 113

Methods 124 

Data acquisition 125 

All KEGG 9–11 proteins, along with the orthology database, were downloaded from the KEGG 126 

FTP server 27 . KEGG data used to train the model for evaluation was downloaded on May 14 th ,127 

2021, and KEGG data used to test the model and to train the latest version of the model was 128 

downloaded on May 24 th , 2023. PANTHER versions 18 and 19 sequences and annotations were 129 

downloaded from the PANTHER database website. UniRef90 28 protein sequences and ID 130 

mappings used to generate the InterPro, EggNOG, and OrthoDB models were downloaded on 131 

January 14 th , 2024 from the UniRef FTP server 29 .132 

Generating unlabeled sequence sets 133 

FAMUS uses unlabeled sequences for training and inference to label sequences as either one 134 

of the previously seen protein families or as “unknown”. For KEGG, these sequences were 135 

taken from the KEGG Genes database if they were not assigned to any KEGG Orthology label. 136 

For PANTHER, UniProt KnowledgeBase IDs of unannotated sequences from the organisms 137 

annotated by PANTHER were gathered (according to https://pantherdb.org for PANTHER 19 138 

and https://archive.pantherdb.org for PANTHER 18). Each unannotated sequence’s UniProt 139 

KnowledgeBase ID was mapped to a UniRef90 sequence. For InterPro, OrthoDB, and the 140 

EggNOG models, these were UniRef90 sequences whose UniProt ID was not mapped to any 141 

InterPro family, OrthoDB family, and any COG, KOG, or arCOG family, respectively. 142 

Generating sub-families and pHMMs 143 

We created two model versions for each database: a comprehensive version with ortholog 144 

families clustered into sub-families and a “light” version where only exceptionally large 145 

ortholog families were clustered into sub-families (see below), resulting in substantially 146 

smaller pHMM databases. To create sub-families, we sub-clustered orthologous groups using 147 

mmseqs2 30 cluster (version bad16c765aac60d84a8fde3548adbb06b34980bd). Each set of 148 

sequences was clustered twice, as detailed below. The first clustering aimed to reduce sequence 149

The pipeline to partition families into sub-families is as follows. For each family: 152 

1. If the family contains fewer than five sequences, skip sub-clustering of this family. 153 

2. Remove redundant sequences: Cluster all the protein sequences using mmseqs2 cluster 154 

with parameters sensitivity=7.5, coverage=0.8, and sequence-identity=0.9. 155 

If the data are being processed for the light version of the model, and this is not an 156 

extensive family (specifically, the length of the longest representative sequence times the 157 

number of representative sequences is less than 500,000,000), skip the next steps and use 158 

all representative sequences as a single cluster representing the family. 159 

3. Cluster the representative sequences to sub-families using mmseqs2 cluster with 160 

parameters sensitivity=7.5 and coverage=0.5. 161 

4. Sort the resulting clusters by the number of sequences. Starting with the largest cluster in 162 

descending order, create a sub-family from each cluster until the cumulative number of 163 

sequences in the sub-families covers at least 80% of the representative sequences. 164 

5. Generate pHMMs for each sub-family that passed the previous step. If any clusters remain 165 

after covering 80% of the representatives (“leftover” sequences), search these sequences 166 

against the existing pHMMs and add each sequence to the sub-family of the pHMM with 167 

the highest bit score. 168 

6. If any “leftover” sequences did not match any profile (no search hits), create a single sub-169 

family comprised of these sequences. 170 

Sub-families with fewer than six sequences were artificially augmented by constructing a 171 

pHMM and using it to generate sequences from the profile using hmmemit (HMMER version 172 

3.3.2) with default parameters. The purpose of this augmentation step is to increase the sample 173 

size of extremely small subclusters, allowing them to be split into three sets that are required 174 

for downstream steps. After the augmentation of small subclusters, pHMMs were constructed 175 

for each subcluster using hmmbuild. The number of sequences, families, and sub-families in 176 

each dataset is detailed in Supplementary Table S1. 177 

In addition to KEGG and PANTHER pHMMs, we created pHMMs from UniRef90 sequences 178 

annotated with InterPro 13 families, OrthoDB 15 families with 200 or more sequences, and COGs, 179 

KOGs, and arCOGs families. To assign sequences to orthologous groups of these databases, 180 

we used mappings between UniRef90 and each database that were acquired from the UniRef 181 

FTP site 28 . All pHMMs were constructed by performing multiple alignments using MAFFT 31

To reduce potential bias induced by extremely large sub-families and decrease the hmmsearch 186 

runtime during model training, we created the training set for KEGG by randomly sampling up 187 

to 60 sequences from each family or sub-family (PANTHER families/sub-families were not 188 

sub-sampled due to the smaller size of this database). We further sampled unlabeled sequences 189 

from the non-redundant set to achieve equal numbers of labeled and unlabeled examples for 190 

both KEGG and PANTHER. The sub-family pHMMs were used to scan the training set using 191 

hmmsearch (HMMER 32 version 3.3.2) default parameters. HMMER reports two types of bit 192 

scores: the full bit score, which is calculated by comparing the entire sequence to the pHMM, 193 

and the best domain bit score. Based on model performance, we chose to use the best domain 194 

bit scores as input. After running hmmsearch, the results were parsed to extract the best domain 195 

bit scores of each query against all the sub-families’ pHMMs. The result of this step is an 196 

𝑁 × 𝑀 matrix, where 𝑁 is the number of sampled sequences and 𝑀 is the number of sub-197 

families, which serves as the input for our model. To avoid data leakage, it was imperative to 198 

avoid using sequences that were part of the pHMM generation. To that end, we adopted the 199 

strategy used by KofamKoala: each sub-family was split into three equally sized groups (± one 200 

sequence). A pHMM was created based on two of the three subgroups, and this pHMM was 201 

used to score the sequences of the third subgroup. This process was repeated for every third of 202 

the sub-family to acquire an unbiased bit score, representing its resemblance to its own sub-203 

family. Bit scores for sequence-profile pairs that were not reported in hmmsearch were regarded 204 

as zero. 205 

Model architecture 206 

We trained a neural network (architecture depicted in Fig. 1) with a Supervised Contrastive 207 

(SupCon) 19 loss function to create protein embeddings such that sequences of the same label 208 

were close to each other in the embedding space. Bit score vectors from the preprocessing 209 

phase are fed into the network to generate low-dimensional projections of the input data. The 210 

goal of SupCon is to optimize the parameters to minimize the distance between two instances 211 

with the same label and maximize the distance between instances with different labels. As a 212 

result, samples from the same class tend to be co-located within the embedding space. A critical 213 

challenge in protein function annotation is OOD detection, i.e., identifying when a protein does 214 

not belong to any family in the reference database. This is particularly important for 215 

frameworks that support custom models trained on arbitrary databases of a restricted functional 216 

domain. To improve OOD detection, we incorporated into the SupCon batches unlabeled 217 

protein sequences as negative examples alongside labeled training data. 218

Sample inference and performance evaluation 254 

To infer labels of input sequences after training, it was necessary to contrast their model-255 

generated embeddings to those of functionally annotated sequences. For this purpose, all 256 

sequences used for training were transformed to embeddings using the model after training was 257 

completed. Input sequences for annotation were transformed to bit score vectors with the same 258 

pHMMs used for training. These vectors were then encoded using the trained model, and the 259 

Euclidean distance between the input embedding and that of every training sequence was 260 

calculated. If the nearest neighbor is assigned to a family, and its distance from the input 261 

sequence encoding is less than a pre-calculated threshold, the input sequence is assigned to the 262 

same family (or families, if the nearest neighbor belongs to more than one family). Otherwise, 263 

the sequence was labeled as “unknown”. The global threshold is calculated prior to 264 

classification using the trained network in three-fold cross-validation: The training data is split 265 

into three, and for each fold, one of the three splits serves as the validation data, and the other 266 

two make up the new training data. The shortest distance between each training sample and 267 

any validation sample is calculated, and the selected threshold for the validation set is the one 268 

that maximizes the weighted F1 score in the classification of the validation dataset. The final 269 

global threshold used was the average of the three selected thresholds. In the inference stage, 270 

FAMUS annotates sequences using the original protein family labels, and not sub-family 271 

membership. 272

Results 296 

FAMUS addresses three key challenges of pHMM-based annotation: (1) it leverages all 297 

similarity scores against the full profile array rather than relying solely on the best hit; (2) it 298 

accurately annotates even sparsely represented families through few-shot contrastive learning; 299 

and (3) it identifies out-of-scope sequences without requiring a user-defined threshold, by 300 

incorporating unannotated sequences as negative examples during training. As an initial test 301 

case, we applied FAMUS to KEGG Orthology annotation (Fig. 2). 302

Data preprocessing 305 

As a preprocessing phase, highly similar sequences within each protein family were filtered 306 

out to remove redundancies due to representation biases in the database (Fig. 3). Next, each 307 

non-redundant sequence was sub-clustered to generate high-resolution protein sub-families 308 

(Fig. 4), and a pHMM was created for each sub-family. The gene embedding for the training 309 

set was generated by sampling sequences from each sub-family and scoring them against all 310 

the pHMMs. The vector of bit score per pHMM was used as the numeric representation for the 311 

neural network’s input. 312 

To prevent data leakage, sequences should not be scored using profiles derived from them. To 313 

that end, we adopted the strategy used by KofamKoala 7: each sub-family was split into three 314 

equally sized groups (±1 sequence). A pHMM was created based on two of the three subgroups, 315 

and was used to score the sequences in the third subgroup (which was not used to build the 316 

pHMM). This process was repeated three times to acquire an unbiased bit score representing 317 

the sequence's resemblance to its own sub-family. 318

Classification 324 

To label new input sequences, first, their similarity scores to the full set of sub-family profiles 325 

were acquired. Using these scores, the neural network generated embeddings in a lower-326 

dimensional space. For the KEGG model, this meant reducing the input from a vector of 38,628 327 

bit-scores, one for each KO sub-family pHMM, to a 320-dimensional space. During the 328 

inference phase, the network was used to generate embeddings for individual sequences. The 329 

label prediction for a sequence was based on its nearest neighbor in the embedding space. 330 

Sequences were classified as ‘unknown’ if their embeddings were closest to an unlabeled 331 

training sample or if their nearest neighbor was further away than an empirically precalculated 332 

global threshold (see Methods). 333 

Since the pHMM search phase was the primary computational bottleneck for the classification 334 

times, we created a light version of the model using fewer pHMMs to improve computational 335 

efficiency for large-scale analyses. In this light version, each protein family (KO in the case of 336 

KEGG, PANTHER family in the case of PANTHER) was represented by a single pHMM, 337 

instead of being clustered into multiple sub-families. This lower-resolution approach 338 

significantly reduced computation time, while maintaining robust accuracy, making it 339 

particularly suitable for analyzing very large datasets. 340

Training additional FAMUS models 405 

Following our approach’s performance, we used the same framework to train models for 406 

additional knowledge bases, namely InterPro families 13 , OrthoDB 15 , and EggNOG 14 . To 407 

provide easy access to our framework, we created a user-friendly web server that allows 408 

annotating FASTA files, available currently at https://app.famus.bursteinlab.org. These allow 409 

users to classify each of the input proteins based on combinations of the four trained databases 410 

using comprehensive or lightweight models: KEGG 9–11 (38,627 comprehensive model families, 411 

24,784 light model families), InterPro 13 (102,590 comprehensive and 25,173 light model 412 

families), OrthoDB 15 (67,414 comprehensive and 26,865 light model families), and EggNOG 14 

413 

(70,417 comprehensive and 21,067 light model families). The results are presented in a tabular 414 

manner where sequences are assigned to a protein family out of each of the selected models, 415 

or annotated as “unknown” if classified as such by the model. Each column may be sorted and 416 

filtered to exclude certain results, and the (filtered or unfiltered) results may be downloaded 417 

directly. For large datasets and expert users, we open-sourced our code and provided all the 418 

pHMM databases compiled as part of this study. These high-resolution pHMM databases for 419 

KEGG, InterPro, OrthoDB, COG, KOG, and arCOG are publicly available at 420 

https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.14941373 and can be integrated into any HMM-based 421 

annotation pipeline independently of FAMUS. 422 

Finally, we created a bioconda package named “famus” which provides the capability to 423 

download and install the aforementioned models locally, train custom models based on an 424 

arbitrary collection of protein families (with or without automatic protein family subclustering) 425 

and negative examples, and annotate protein sequences using any combination of pre-made or 426 

custom FAMUS models. Installation and usage instructions are provided in 427 

https://github.com/burstein-lab/famus. 428 

Discussion 429 

We present FAMUS, Functional Annotation Method Using Supervised contrastive learning, 430 

which transforms protein functional annotation through a novel contrastive learning approach. 431 

By recasting the traditional annotation problem as a relational inference task, FAMUS 432 

overcomes fundamental challenges in genome-scale functional assignment, particularly the 433 

limited availability of training data for many protein families. Our framework begins with 434 

automated clustering of protein families to capture their inherent diversity, generating multiple 435 

profile Hidden Markov Models (pHMMs) that provide high-resolution similarity scores. These 436 

refined scores are then processed by a compact neural network, combining the sensitivity of 437 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted July 7, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.08.710366 doi: bioRxiv preprint

pHMMs with the discriminative power of contrastive learning to achieve both high accuracy 438 

and sufficient speed for large-scale metagenomic analysis. Some databases, such as 439 

PANTHER 23 , do partition genes into sub-families, which are typically more homogeneous than 440 

the general families they comprise. Standardizing the use of a single computational tool (such 441 

as MMseqs2) to generate sub-families based on sequence similarity is an efficient and effective 442 

way to ensure that the pHMM landscapes derived from various databases lend themselves well 443 

to our framework, which emphasizes minimal user intervention. FAMUS directly addresses the 444 

three key limitations of pHMM-based annotation identified in the Introduction: best-hit 445 

reliance, low-specificity profiles for diverse families, and the difficulty of setting robust 446 

annotation thresholds at scale. 447 

FAMUS was developed for general annotation, and it is thus applicable to any set of protein 448 

families and well-suited to annotating entire genomes and metagenomes. Our benchmarking 449 

results demonstrate that FAMUS is able to avoid misclassifying proteins while maintaining a 450 

high level of recall, a property that is particularly important when annotating metagenomes of 451 

understudied environments and species. Furthermore, FAMUS is modular by design, allowing 452 

seamless integration of various annotation databases and enabling users to leverage 453 

complementary information. This cross-database capability provides more comprehensive 454 

annotation coverage while maintaining consistency across different annotation sources. In 455 

addition, the streamlined and lightweight design of the FAMUS model allows for scalability, 456 

annotating millions of sequences in a short timeframe. 457 

Like all machine learning approaches, FAMUS has inherent limitations in its ability to make 458 

novel functional predictions. While it can identify subtle and complex similarity patterns, the 459 

model still relies on its training data to make informative annotations. Additionally, practical 460 

constraints necessitated certain compromises in database coverage. For InterPro 13 , for example, 461 

our current model focused solely on families while excluding superfamilies to avoid an 462 

explosive growth in the number of sub-families. In the case of OrthoDB 15 , we included only 463 

groups with 200 or more members (26,865 families), and for EggNOG 14 , we restricted our 464 

scope to COG, KOG, and arCOG groups (21,067 families), excluding eNOGs. These choices 465 

were driven by balancing computational feasibility and output usefulness. 466 

Future improvements to FAMUS could include a ranking system and confidence levels for 467 

predicted annotations. Such a system would let users filter predictions based on their specific 468 

confidence requirements, enabling flexible tradeoffs between annotation coverage and 469 

reliability. Owing to the modularity of our framework, users can produce different databases in 470 

various scopes, granularities, and for different domains. Additionally, with sufficient 471 

computational resources, exploring FAMUS's performance on complete ortholog databases, 472 

including domains and uncharacterized proteins, could reveal the full potential of our approach 473 

for comprehensive annotation and organization of protein families. 474
