Abstract 12 

Collective foraging is widespread across the animal kingdom, allowing animals to 13 

more effectively discover resources. However, collective foragers need to balance a 14 

key trade off between private exploration and using social information. Social in-15 

formation can come in very distinct forms, ranging from simple positional cues to 16 

complex payoff information. However, how the types of available social cues and 17 

environmental volatility shape collective foraging behavior is not well understood. 18 

We address this using a spatially-explicit model in which agents track a mobile 19 

resource via multi-agent reinforcement learning. Agents choose between random 20 

exploration, private tracking, and social attraction. We systematically varied re-21 

source volatility and the type of available social cues to analyze their effect on 22 

individual and collective behavior. Our results show that the quality of social in-23 

formation dictates the emerging collective behavior. Low-quality social cues (e.g., 24 

positions, actions) result in a fragile strategy that is effective in stable environments 25 

but fails as volatility increases. Conversely, high-quality social information (e.g., 26 

payoffs) enables behavioral diversity: Agents selectively copy others and flexibly 27 

change between individual tracking or exploration depending on the environmen-28 

tal volatility. Our findings identify the interplay between information quality and 29 

ecological context as an important mechanism governing the emergence of distinct 30 

forms of collective behavior from individual decision rules. 31 

Keywords: Collective Behavior; Social Foraging; Social Learning; Multi-Agent Rein-32 

forcement Learning; Agent-based Simulations 33

# 1 Introduction 34 

A fundamental challenge in collective decision-making is determining when to rely on 35 

independent exploration versus when to copy others. Animal social foraging serves as 36 

a prime testbed for understanding this dynamic, as individuals must constantly balance 37 

the costs of discovering resources through private exploration with the benefits of using 38 

socially acquired information from their peers [1–3]. This trade-off between private ex-39 

ploration and the use of social information is a central challenge in behavioral ecology, 40 

as relying on others can provide a valuable shortcut but also risks the propagation of 41 

outdated or erroneous information [4, 5]. 42 

To explain how animals solve this problem, classic models often rely on a fixed set of 43 

heuristics like “copy the successful” or “copy the majority” [6–8]. While there is robust 44 

evidence for the use of such strategies across many contexts, these traditional models 45 

necessarily make specific assumptions about what information social cues provide and 46 

specify predefined functional forms for their integration. As a result, they fail to capture 47 

the processes of how animals flexibly integrate the rich, multidimensional social informa-48 

tion available in their surroundings. Social information can, however, vary from discrete 49 

location cues (e.g., observing the location of others) all the way to graded public infor-50 

mation (e.g., observing foraging success of others) [9, 10]. For instance, a foraging bird 51 

might observe a neighbor’s presence (positional cue), see it actively pecking (action 52 

cue), or witness it successfully extracting grubs (payoff-based public information) [11]. 53 

The adaptive value of each cue is highly context-dependent. In a stable environment, sim-54 

ple cues may already provide useful social information. However, in volatile environments 55 

where resources are ephemeral—such as birds tracking thermal updrafts [12] or predators 56 

hunting mobile prey [13]—simple positional cues often become rapidly outdated, losing 57 

their validity as reliable indicators of resource location. In these contexts, only more 58 

complex social information, such as high-fidelity payoff signals, remains adaptive [14]. A 59 

significant gap remains in understanding how individuals learn to weigh these distinct 60 

cue types based on their reliability and their ecological context. 61 

Deep multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) offers a powerful framework to ad-62 

dress this gap. This approach lets the model learn the functional relationships between 63 

cues directly from experience, enabling the discovery of novel strategies that researchers 64 

might not have anticipated. Treating agents as “smart particles” whose decisions and 65 

movements are optimized together [15] allows MARL to generate flexible, adaptive poli-66 

cies through reward maximization. This supports recent efforts to adopt more holistic 67 

views on social learning and collective intelligence [16, 17]. Prior work has shown that 68 

MARL agents can learn biologically plausible behaviors, such as using social information 69 

adaptively [18], converging on collective foraging patterns [19, 20], and spontaneously 70 

acquiring social learning heuristics [21]. Our work builds on this by providing a prin-71

We model a social foraging scenario where agents track a mobile resource performing a 75 

correlated random walk (Fig. 1A), mimicking the directional persistence and path tortu-76 

osity observed in many biological prey species [e.g., 22]. The agents’ behavioral repertoire 77 

(Fig. 1B) is inspired by fundamental strategies observed across diverse animal species. 78 

Agents can engage in a random walk ( exploration ), a widely observed search pattern 79 

when animals lack reliable directional cues [23]. Alternatively, agents can use their private 80 

information to track the resource. This action is modeled as inherently costly, result-81 

ing in a slower effective tracking speed than other actions. This cost operationalizes the 82 

energetic and cognitive demands of actively sensing and pursuing a resource, such as a 83 

predator stalking prey [24], and reflects behavioral constraints like pausing to sense or 84 

orient, a constraint central to gradient-following strategies like infotaxis [25, 26]. Finally, 85 

agents can move toward a peer ( social attraction ), a core mechanism of social learning 86 

where individuals join others to exploit a resource discovered by other agents [11, 27]. 87 

Although we acknowledge that acquiring social information—particularly complex payoff 88 

signals—can involve significant cognitive and opportunity costs in nature [1], we here 89 

choose to model social acquisition as a relatively low-cost alternative to private tracking. 90 

This simplification serves to more clearly isolate how different cue types shape the in-91 

terplay between individual sensing, social information use, and random exploration (the 92 

latter acting as a “zero-information” search baseline), while recognizing that real-world 93 

scenarios may involve more balanced or context-specific costs for social learning. This 94 

action set allows us to probe the fundamental trade-off between relying on costly indi-95 

vidual sensing, using socially acquired information, and engaging in uninformed search. 96 

To investigate the optimal group-level strategies that emerge from these trade-offs, our 97 

primary MARL approach uses centralized training, in which agents share neural net-98 

work weights. This method is known to effectively find such group-level solutions (which 99 

emerge from optimized individual decision rules). We also explored a fully decentralized 100 

version, where each agent learns independently using its own distinct network. We sys-101 

tematically vary both the quality of social cues (from neighbor distance to noisy payoff 102 

signals) and the environmental volatility (resource speed), to study how their interplay 103 

drives emergent collective foraging behavior. 104 

Our results show that the quality of available social information shapes the emergence 105 

of individual-level decision rules as well as the collective performance. And that envi-106 

ronmental volatility shapes the adaptive value of social cues. In stable, slow-changing 107 

environments, agents limited to low-quality social cues converged on a Cohesive Track-108 

ing strategy. This strategy achieved near-maximal performance (Fig. 2) by predomi-109 

nantly relying on costly private tracking supplemented with occasional social attraction 110

## 2.1 The Collective Foraging Model 126 

2.1.1 Environment and Resource Dynamics 127 

To investigate how social information quality and environmental volatility shape the 128 

emergence of individual-level decision rules, we frame collective foraging as a resource-129 

tracking problem where agents are rewarded for tracking a mobile resource (Fig. 1A). The 130 

task takes place in a continuous 20 × 20 unit square arena A with reflective boundaries. 131 

A group of N = 10 identical agents tracks a single mobile resource for T = 1000 discrete 132 

time steps. 133 

Let xR(t) ∈ A be the resource’s position at time t. The movement of the resource 134 

follows a correlated random walk. Turning angles θ are sampled uniformly from [0 , 2π).135 

Step lengths ℓ are drawn from a truncated Pareto distribution with a shape parameter α =136 

1 and 1 ≤ ℓ ≤ 100 . The resource travels at a constant speed vresource which controls the 137 

task’s overall difficulty (see Section 2.3). This truncated power-law distribution generates 138 

occasional long relocations, mixing local diffusion with long-distance movements. While 139 

not a formal Lévy walk (as truncation ensures finite moments), this pattern characterizes 140 

real-world animal and resource movements across a wide range of taxa [31–33]. Tracking 141 

this erratic target encapsulates the ecological challenge of following spatiotemporally 142 

dynamic resources that underlies various types of foraging [24, 34]. 143 

All agents and the resource were initialized randomly for each training and evaluation 144 

episode within a 10 × 10 area in the center of the environment to prevent agents and the 145 

resource from starting near the arena boundaries. 146

2.2.2 Agent Observations 196 

Agents operate under partial observability, receiving only local information. To model 197 

both private information and the different types of social cues (distance, action, payoff), 198 

each agent i perceives its environment through a five-dimensional observation vector oi(t),199 

showed schematically in Fig. 1B. This is constructed based on a visual range rvis = 15 200 

units (see Section 2.5 for robustness checks). 201 

Let Vi(t) = {j̸ = i | ∥ xj (t) − xi(t)∥2 ≤ rvis } be the set of peers visible to agent 202 

i at time t. To model perceptual and attentional constraints often observed in animal 203 

groups [e.g. 37], and to simplify the analysis by avoiding complex social information 204 

aggregation rules, if Vi(t) is not empty, agent i attends to exactly one peer j, sampled 205 

uniformly at random from Vi(t) at each time step. While animals in nature typically bias 206 

their attention toward closer neighbors, this uniform sampling—bounded by a maximum 207 

visual range—simplifies our analysis by avoiding complex distance-weighted aggregation 208 

rules. The agent then uses this focal peer’s information to decide whether to approach 209 

it (via the Social Attraction action) or choose a different behavior. If Vi(t) is empty, no 210 

social information is gathered. Because the focal neighbor is resampled independently at 211 

each time step, the attention process is ergodic: given enough time, an agent will observe 212 

each group member with equal frequency. This corresponds to an effective averaging of 213 

interactions across neighbors over time. Thus, our qualitative results are representative 214 

of a setting where each agent observes all neighbors simultaneously in the limit of a 215 

slowly changing environment, analogous to results on synchronization in blinking network 216 

models [38, 39]. 217

## 2.4 Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) 253 

2.4.1 Training Algorithm 254 

We adopt a MARL approach to train agents to maximize their individual rewards, al-255 

lowing collective strategies to emerge. Specifically, we used Multi-Agent Proximal Policy 256 

Optimization (MAPPO) [40, 41], a widely used algorithm based on the centralized train-257 

ing–decentralized execution (CTDE) paradigm. CTDE is well-suited for partially ob-258 

servable multi-agent problems; the centralized critic leverages global information during 259 

training to stabilize learning and improve coordination [42], while the decentralized actors 260 

learn policies based only on local observations, ensuring scalability during execution. All 261 

agents shared the parameters of a single actor policy network and a single centralized 262 

critic network. 263

with two hidden layers of 256 units each, using tanh activation functions. The actor 266

## 2.6 Data Collection and Evaluation Procedure 309 

2.6.1 Performance Evaluation 310 

Following best practices for RL reproducibility [50], each experimental condition was 311 

trained using seven independent random seeds. Learned policies were evaluated every 312 

20 training iterations during the final phase of training (iterations 400 to 480) to assess 313 

performance near convergence. Each evaluation consisted of 1000 independent rollout 314 

episodes per seed. This resulted in 5 evaluation points × 1000 episodes/point = 5000 315 

evaluation episodes per seed. Final performance metrics (e.g., average reward) reported 316 

in the Results are averaged across these 5000 episodes and then across the 7 seeds. Error 317 

bars represent 95% confidence intervals computed across the 7 independent seeds. 318 

2.6.2 Strategy Consistency Analysis 319 

To confirm that the training process consistently converged to similar behavioral strate-320 

gies across different random seeds within the same condition, we performed a post-hoc 321 

analysis of behavioral strategy similarity. For each condition, we first calculated the aver-322 

age action composition vector (proportion of time spent in Exploration, Tracking, Social 323 

Attraction, analogous to the behavioral analysis described in the Results Section 3.2) for 324 

each of the 7 seeds, averaging across all 5000 evaluation episodes for that seed. We then 325 

computed the pairwise cosine similarity between these 7 strategy vectors. 326 

Agglomerative hierarchical clustering was performed on the cosine distance matrix ( 1−327 

similarity) derived from these vectors. We examined the first bifurcation (split at the root) 328 

of the clustering tree. If the average cosine similarity between the two resulting clusters 329 

fell below a threshold of 0.9, we flagged the smaller cluster as representing a divergent or 330 

potentially unconverged strategy. In such cases, the runs belonging to the smaller cluster 331 

were excluded from the main analysis to ensure reported results reflect the dominant, 332 

consistent behavioral mode. This conservative pruning procedure, implemented using 333 

SciPy [51] and Scikit-learn [52], affected only 5 out of 147 total training runs (approx. 334 

3.5%), mostly in non-social conditions. Overall consistency within the retained dominant 335 

clusters was very high (average cosine similarity > 0.95 ), confirming the robustness of 336 

the emergent strategies (for details see Supplementary Information Table S3). 337

# 3 Results 349 

We present our findings as follows. We first establish that the adaptive value of any given 350 

social cue is context-dependent, with high-quality payoff signals becoming essential as en-351 

vironmental volatility increases. We then explore how these performance differences arise 352 

from shifts in individual agent behavior, leading to the emergence of collective tracking 353 

strategies. Finally, we confirm that these emergent strategies are robust outcomes of the 354 

agent-environment interaction by testing their stability against changes in both intrinsic 355 

costs and the learning algorithm itself. 356

## 3.3 Emergence of distinct collective behaviors 418 

The shifts in individual behavior give rise to distinct, group-level strategies. To illustrate 419 

these findings, Fig. 4 visualizes the learned spatial dynamics and decision rules for these 420 

contrasting strategies, while Fig. 5 provides a conceptual summary and demonstrates 421 

how strategy selection is driven by tracking viability. As summarized by the conceptual 422 

flowchart in Fig. 5A, the quality of social information is the primary determinant of 423 

the group’s behavioral flexibility. Low-quality social cues constrain the group to a single 424 

strategy, whereas high-quality social cues enable a flexible response based on the viability 425 

of private tracking. Below, we detail the three major emergent strategies, focusing on the 426 

most volatile environment ( vresource = 0 .5 × vmax ). 427 

Cohesive Tracking When agents only have access to low-quality social information 428 

(e.g., neighbor distance), they are locked into a single strategy: Cohesive Tracking. In this 429 

mode, agents predominantly rely on private tracking while using social attraction non-430

a peer unless it is already very close to the resource’s center or the peer itself is very 432 

closeby (Fig. 4, top panels; Supplementary Videos 1). This results in a compact, mobile 433 

cluster that moves as a coherent unit, reminiscent of collective movements where leaders 434 

“pull” followers along [54]. While effective in predictable environments, the strategy’s 435 

strong reliance on private tracking means its performance deteriorates when the resource 436 

becomes more volatile (Fig. 3) or the costs of tracking increase due to lower effective 437 

tracking speed relative to resource speed (Fig. 2 and Fig. S2). 438 

Track-or-Copy When high-quality payoff information is available and private tracking 439 

is viable (i.e., tracking cost is low due to greater effective tracking speed relative to 440 

resource speed), a more straightforward Track-or-Copy strategy emerges. Here, agents 441 

still default to private tracking but now use social information selectively. By observing 442 

a neighbor’s payoff at each time step, they follow a rule akin to the “copy the successful” 443 

heuristic [7]: track privately, but if a more successful neighbor with a higher payoff is 444 

detected, copy them. This decision rule is clearly visible as a diagonal boundary in their 445 

action probability map (Fig. 5B, bottom-left panel; Supplementary Videos 2): agents 446 

choose social attraction only when the observed peer is closer to the resource center (i.e., 447 

has a higher payoff) than themselves; otherwise, they default to private tracking. This 448 

represents a more efficient use of social information than simple cohesion. 449 

Explore-or-Copy and Distributed Sensing When high-quality payoff information 450 

is available but private tracking is rendered ineffective by high environmental volatility 451 

(i.e., the cost of tracking increases due to lower effective tracking speed relative to re-452 

source speed), the group flexibly switches to an Explore-or-Copy strategy. While this 453 

strategy uses the same social rule as Track-or-Copy (i.e., copy a more successful peer), it 454 

is fundamentally different in its default asocial behavior. Agents abandon costly tracking 455 

altogether and instead engage in random exploration. Put simply, if a neighbor with 456 

a higher payoff is detected, copy them; if not, explore randomly (Fig. 4, bottom pan-457 

els; Fig. 5B, bottom-right panel Supplementary Videos 3). This individual rule gives 458 

rise to a powerful emergent behavior that functions as a form of distributed collective 459 

sensing [12, 29, 30]. Successful explorers become temporary information hubs, attracting 460 

other agents and allowing the group as a whole to effectively track the resource in a 461 

decentralized manner. 462

# 4 Discussion 506 

Our study demonstrates how environmental volatility and the availability and type of 507 

social information interactively shape the emergence of collective foraging strategies. By 508 

systematically varying the type and fidelity of social features in a simulated social foraging 509 

18 

.CC-BY 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. value within specific ecological contexts. We identified two distinct behavioral regimes 511 

determined by information quality. The first is a fragile, cohesive strategy reliant on low-512 

quality social cues. The second is flexible and adaptive, emerging only when high-quality 513 

social information is available. 514 

With only access to low-quality social cues (neighbor proximity or action), agents 515 

converged on a Cohesive Tracking strategy. This strategy, effective in stable, slow-moving 516 

resource environments, relies on all agents predominantly using costly private tracking 517 

while using social cues to maintain a cohesive group. However, this strategy was not 518 

robust. Its success was highly sensitive to ecological context; performance degraded 519 

significantly as environmental volatility (resource speed) increased or the cost of private 520 

tracking increased (i.e., lower effective vtracking ; see Fig. 5B). 521 

In sharp contrast, access to high-quality payoff information allowed the group to 522 

be more flexible in their behavior. This enabled them to switch strategies based on 523 

the viability of private tracking. When private tracking was effective (slow resource or 524 

low tracking cost), agents adopted a Track-or-Copy strategy, using social information to 525 

selectively copy more successful peers. When private tracking became ineffective (fast 526 

resource or high tracking cost), the group evolved an Explore-or-Copy strategy. This 527 

emergent behavior functions as a form of distributed sensing, where agents abandon costly 528 

tracking, explore randomly, and use payoff cues to aggregate at temporary information 529 

hubs created by successful explorers (Fig. 4). 530 

The emergence of the “Explore-or-Copy” strategy highlights a flexible dynamic related 531 

to the classic Producer-Scrounger (P-S) dilemma [1, 55–58]. It is important to note that 532 

traditional P-S models typically assume stationary, depleting resources where individuals 533 

actively compete, creating a strict dilemma around information sharing. While our model 534 

features a mobile, non-depleting resource–thereby removing direct competition over the 535 

resource itself–it nonetheless captures the core informational trade-off of the P-S frame-536 

work. In our model, agents are not locked into fixed “Producer” (Exploration/Tracking) 537 

or “Scrounger” (Social Attraction) roles. Rather, the availability of high-fidelity payoff 538 

information enables them to dynamically adopt the most profitable role at any given 539 

time. An agent can act as a producer by exploring. The instant an agent observes a more 540 

successful peer, they switch to the scrounger role to copy that peer. This emergent behav-541 

ior closely mirrors empirical evidence showing that animals conditionally rely on social 542 

information during foraging, by primarily using it when they are unsuccessful themselves 543 

[6, 59–62]. Such selectivity makes strategies based on high-quality social information not 544 

only robust to ecological factors (like volatility) but also robust to social uncertainty. 545 

The group becomes effectively “immune” to the dilemma’s primary pitfall: the risk of 546 

scroungers copying other unsuccessful scroungers, leading to negative information cas-547 

cades [53, 63]. Because successful agents are reliably identified, the incentive to explore 548

This makes agents vulnerable to copying errors, which degrades performance. The highly 553

decentralized training experiments supported this, showing that performance in this low-557 

quality social information regime dropped significantly without a mechanism to establish 558 

this group-wide behavioral consensus. 559 

The emergence and viability of these strategies depend on the assumption that indi-560 

vidual tracking is more costly than using social information. This asymmetry is essential 561 

to generating the central trade-off that we aimed to study. If social learning were equally 562 

or more expensive than private tracking, the incentive to copy others would disappear, 563 

resulting in purely asocial behavior. It is important to note, however, that operational-564 

izing these relative costs when modeling biological systems is crucial, yet highly non-565 

trivial. “Cost” can be expressed in various ways: as temporal delays (as implemented 566 

in our study), reduced precision of private information, or direct energetic costs. At 567 

the same time, acquiring high-fidelity public information, such as accurately assessing a 568 

conspecific’s exact payoff, could also impose its own significant perceptual and cognitive 569 

demands in many naturalistic scenarios. If these social acquisition costs outweigh the 570 

energetic burden of private sensing, we would expect a higher threshold for using social 571 

information, which would likely drive groups toward independent search strategies. 572 

From a broader theoretical perspective, the learned strategies functionally encode the 573 

value of information, reflecting an adaptive valuation based on its utility in reducing 574 

uncertainty [2, 64]. In our model, simple, low-bandwidth cues–analogous to observing 575 

a neighbor’s mere presence–are sufficient to reduce uncertainty in stable environments; 576 

indeed, even when high-bandwidth, high-fidelity signals–analogous to observing a peer’s 577 

actual foraging success–are available, private tracking is still preferred. However, as en-578 

vironmental volatility increases, relying on simple cues becomes less adaptive, and high-579 

fidelity signals become necessary to sustain collective performance. It has been argued, 580 

however, that in many natural systems, animals often only have access to simple behav-581 

ioral cues rather than explicit, payoff-based information, making groups vulnerable to 582 

suboptimal informational cascades [e.g., 53]. Our results show that when high-quality 583 

social cues are unavailable and the environment is highly volatile, social information be-584 

comes fundamentally unreliable. In such contexts, the best an individual can do is refrain 585 

from using social cues entirely and rely instead on private information. Our findings thus 586 

mechanistically demonstrate that adaptive foraging requires constantly balancing infor-587

Using deep reinforcement learning, our study establishes a normative baseline for 590

We investigated optimal social information usage in a simplified environment with 598 

a single moving resource. Future work could extend this scenario in several directions. 599 

First, exploring multi-resource scenarios could provide valuable insights into collective 600 

shepherding [65, 66] and collective hunting [34] behaviors. Second, investigating hetero-601

multiple peers simultaneously [73]–would be a critical step toward understanding more 614 

complex attentional mechanisms and collective decision-making, better capturing the rich 615 

social dynamics observed in natural systems. 616
