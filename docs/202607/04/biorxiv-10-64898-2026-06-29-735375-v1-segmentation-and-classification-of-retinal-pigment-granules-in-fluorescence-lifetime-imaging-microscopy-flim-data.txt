12 Abstract 

13 Alterations of fluorescence properties in retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells caused by diseases such as age-related macular 

14 degeneration (AMD) highlight the need for detailed analysis of the fluorescent RPE granules at the individual level. Precise 

15 segmentation and classification of these granules remain challenging due to their limited visual separability. In this study, we 

16 present Classi4RPE, a computational algorithm designed to accurately segment RPE granules and classify them into three 

17 categories — lipofuscin (L), melanolipofuscin (ML), and melanin (M) — based on fluorescence lifetime imaging data, which 

18 provide distinctive contrast. The method is implemented in a custom Python framework and employs seeded watershed 

19 segmentation to isolate individual granules. Lipofuscin granules are identified as hyperfluorescent structures with longer 

20 lifetimes, while granules with shorter lifetimes are further analyzed based on their spatial lifetime distribution from the center 

21 to edge, enabling discrimination of ML from other melanin-rich granules. Our approach achieves high performance, with mean 

22 sensitivities of 0.99 for L granules and 0.90 for ML granules, and corresponding specificities of 0.93 and 0.98, respectively, 

23 compared to manually annotated ground truth. These results demonstrate the potential of Classi4RPE to surpass human visual 

24 limitations and provide a robust tool for quantitative RPE analysis. 

25 Introduction 

26 Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE) granules are intracellular pigment accumulations formed from non ‑degradable phagocytic 

27 by ‑products. They are generated during the daily phagocytosis of photoreceptor outer segments as well as visual pigment 

28 recycling in the human retina [1] . Because the RPE serves as a critical metabolic and transport interface between the choroid 

29 and the neural retina [2] , [3] , these granules contribute to structural and functional alterations associated with several retinal 

30 diseases [2], including age ‑related macular degeneration (AMD) [2] , [4] . RPE granules are commonly categorized into three 

31 major types based on their autofluorescence properties and morphology. Lipofuscins (L) exhibit strong autofluorescence and 

32 typically display irregular, near ‑spherical shapes with heterogeneous fluorescence distributions. Melanin ‑rich granules, or 

33 melanosomes (M), show minimal autofluorescence in the visible range but stronger emission in the near ‑infrared (NIR) 

34 spectrum [1] , [5] , and their morphology ranges from spherical to spindle ‑like. Melanolipofuscins (ML) contain both melanin 

35 and lipofuscin components; they characteristically exhibit a ring ‑like fluorescence pattern (melanin ‑rich core with fluorescent 

36 periphery) and may appear either spherical or spindle ‑shaped.

37 Fluorescence lifetime imaging microscopy (FLIM) provides an additional discriminative parameter that enhances granule 

38 characterization [6], and several recent studies have reported FLIM ‑based lifetime alterations associated with AMD progression 

39 [6], [7], [8], [9]. 

40 Quantifying these granules and understanding their statistical distributions is essential for assessing how biochemical alterations 

41 associated with retinal diseases influence RPE physiology. Advances in imaging and image ‑processing techniques have 

42 recently enabled more detailed clinical understanding of such granule ‑related changes [10]. Analyzing individual granules, 

43 thus, promises to achieve a deeper mechanistic understanding of correlations between granule types and AMD progression. 

44 In this study, we used FLIM images acquired via two ‑photon excitation at 960 nm to perform automated segmentation and 

45 classification of RPE granules. This purpose requires higher resolved images and more efficient segmentation approach at the 

46 granule level, which is not typically addressed in most cellular-level studies. Conventional segmentation approaches previously 

47 applied to biological images [11] were unsuitable for this task because they required extensive manual fine ‑tuning, yielding a 

48 low reproducibility of the results. Similarly, recently developed software tools offering pretrained models for microscopic 

49 images [12], [13] still required manual adjustments for unresolved segments, which remained difficult to delineate due to the 

50 highly variable autofluorescence patterns within individual granules. It was also challenging and time consuming to create 

51 human ‑annotated training data, sufficient for training a neural-network based segmentation, thus limiting the feasibility of such 

52 neural ‑network approaches. 

53 Previous studies have made substantial progress in segmenting retinal layers from optical coherence tomography images [14]. 

54 However, segmentation becomes considerably more challenging in the presence of RPE granules. Yu et al. (2023) applied 

55 semantic segmentation to RPE flat ‑mount images, successfully delineating RPE cells. Their findings highlight how AMD alters 

56 RPE cell borders, making it difficult to separate reliably during segmentation [15]. 

57 In contrast, our AMD-effected cross-sections of RPE images contain hundreds of granules belonging to multiple subclasses, 

58 each strongly affected by disease ‑related changes. This complexity necessitates instance ‑level segmentation to accurately 

59 delineate individual granules. To meet this need, seeded water shed segmentation [16] was applied by introducing seed 

60 assignment for each granule, enabling robust instance segmentation that captures nearly all granules present in the dataset. To 

61 further address these challenges, we developed a customized automated algorithm, Classi4RPE, capable of segmenting RPE 

62 granules and classifying them into three major granule types based on their FLIM characteristics.

63 Methods 

64 RPE samples used 

65 Use of human tissue was approved by institutional review board at the University of Alabama at Birmingham (#N170213002). 

66 Whole eyes of Caucasian donors aged 80 years or older without diabetes mellitus and no reported history of head trauma, 

67 surgeries affecting the retina, or conditions affecting the macula other than AMD were collected within 6 hours of death from 

68 Advancing Sight Network (Birmingham AL USA). After anterior segment removal, eyes were preserved in 4% buffered 

69 paraformaldehyde. Unstained glass slides with 12 μm thick cryosections were shipped on dry ice by overnight courier to Jena 

70 for autofluorescence lifetime microscopy (3-4 slides per eye). 

71 FLIM measurements 

72 Autofluorescence lifetimes were recorded using an inverted multiphoton laser scanning microscope (Axio Observer Z.1 and 

73 LSM 710 NLO, 63x oil immersion objective Plan-Apochromat NA = 1.4, Carl Zeiss, Jena, Germany) in combination with a 

74 femtosecond Ti:Sapphire laser (Chameleon Ultra, Coherent Inc., Santa Clara, CA, repetition rate of 80 MHz with a pulse 

75 duration of 140 fs) and a single photon counting fluorescence lifetime imaging setup (Becker & Hickl GmbH, Berlin, Germany) 

76 with a 20 to 30 ps time resolution, as described [7]. The excitation wavelength for lifetime recordings was set to 960 nm for 

77 two-photon excitation (TPE). The lifetime imaging measurements are based on the principle of time-correlated single photon 

78 counting (TCSPC). The two-photon-excited fluorescence was recorded in two spectral channels. The short-wavelength spectral 

79 channel (SSC) was 500 – 550 nm, and the long-wavelength spectral channel (LSC) was 550 – 700 nm. All FLIM images were 

80 acquired as an average of 100 fast scans (mean photon count per pixel of approximately 1000, pixel dwell time of 3.1 μs) with 

81 a resolution of 256 × 256 pixel and a field of view of 27 × 27 μm. 

82 The fluorescence decay images from the FLIM detectors were analysed using the software SPCImage 8.0 (Becker&Hickl 

83 GmbH, Berlin, Germany). For decay data fitting, a 3x3 pixel binning was applied. The decays where approximated by the 

84 fitting method of maximum likelihood estimation and with a three-exponential model yielding three decay time constants and 

85 three amplitudes.

93 Classi4RPE concept 

94 Lifetime ‑fitted data were exported from the Becker & Hickl GmbH software together with the corresponding intensity images. 

95 The second detection channel (emission >550 nm) was selected for analysis, as it provided better signal quality for the approach 

96 described below; the discussion for this choice is addressed later. Figure 1 summarizes the workflow for RPE granules 

97 segmentation and classification. 

98 1- Division of the image into two sub-images: 

99 The classification of granules is complicated by the heterogeneity of their fluorescence signal. Lipofuscins (L) exhibit 

100 characteristically high fluorescence intensity, whereas Melanosomes (M) produce a comparatively weak signal. The 

101 third granule type, Melanolipofuscin (ML), is a composite structure comprising a weak M-like signal in the center 

102 surrounded by the strong fluorescence characteristic of L. When a segmentation method (seeded watershed 

103 segmentation) is applied directly to the fluorescence intensity image, granules with weak signal (M and ML) are 

104 frequently under-detected, leading to erroneous classification outcomes. To overcome this limitation, we exploited 

105 additional information encoded in the spatial distribution of fluorescence lifetime, specifically the fact that L exhibits 

106 a longer fluorescence lifetime than M. 

107 The fluorescence lifetime histogram (omitting the pixels without any significant intensity signal) was computed, and 

108 a threshold was determined as follows. The lifetime data were assumed to comprise three overlapping clusters, each 

109 described by a Gaussian distribution. A Gaussian mixture model (GMM) [17,18] was fitted to estimate the mean and 

110 standard deviation of each cluster (corresponding to M, L, and their intermediate state). The discriminative threshold 

111 was defined as the mean lifetime of the lowest-lifetime cluster (corresponding to M) plus 25% of its standard deviation. 

112 Pixels with lifetime values exceeding this threshold were assigned to Mask A, in which only L contributions are

113 expected. The remaining pixels, with lifetime values below the threshold, were assigned to Mask B, which 

114 encompasses the M population and the central M-like region of ML granules, as described above. 

115 2- Segmentation of the two sub-images: 

116 Seeded watershed segmentation [16] was applied independently to each of the two masked intensity sub-images (Mask 

117 A and Mask B) to delineate individual granules. Seed points were identified as the local maxima of the difference-of-

118 Gaussians (DoG, σ₁,₂ = 1.6, 3.0) filtered intensity image. To prevent over-segmentation, a minimum inter-seed 

119 distance criterion was enforced: 7 pixels for Mask B (containing M and ML granules) and 3 pixels for Mask A 

120 (containing L granules), consistent with the given image magnification. A watershed algorithm was subsequently 

121 applied to the inverted intensity images, propagating segmentation regions outward from each seed point to delineate 

122 individual granule boundaries. Segmentation was performed using 8-connected neighborhood connectivity. 

123 3- Classification of the granules: 

124 Classification of L granules identified in the Mask A sub-image is straightforward. All cluster in the image are 

125 classified as L. Additionally; each segment is dilated by one pixel to ensure that granule edges are fully captured 

126 within the segmented region. 

127 Classification of M and ML granules from the Mask B sub-image requires additional analysis. Segments are first 

128 dilated by two pixels to incorporate the outer ring of ML granules — a region dominated by L fluorescence and 

129 therefore absent from Mask B — into the subsequent analysis. For each dilated segment, a radial fluorescence lifetime 

130 profile is extracted and fitted with a quadratic function. For M granules, the radial lifetime distribution is spatially 

131 uniform. For ML granules, however, the radial profile exhibits systematically elevated lifetime values at the periphery 

132 relative to the center, reflecting the longer fluorescence lifetime of the L-rich outer shell compared to the M-like core. 

133 For each granule, the ratio of the lifetime at the edge to that at the center (R_τ) is computed. Granules with R_τ < 1.15 

134 are classified as M, while those with R_τ ≥ 1.15 are classified as ML. 

135 4- Image merging (Classi4RPE output image): 

136 The final Classi4RPE output image is reconstructed by combining all classified segments. As a consequence of the 

137 image subdivision and the dilation steps applied during segmentation, boundary overlap between adjacent granule 

138 segments may occur. In the final composite image, each granule type is rendered in a distinct color (L – red, ML – 

139 blue, M – green), and granule edges are highlighted in white to enable unambiguous distinction between adjacent 

140 granules of the same type.

145 Results 

146 Classi4RPE was applied to seven datasets of which three were used to determine optimal threshold values, followed 

147 by evaluation of the overall performance across all datasets. Ground ‑truth images were generated from granule coordinates 

148 identified manually on the segmented images. However, not all granules could be clearly distinguished by eye, and in some 

149 cases the classification was particularly challenging. Consequently, segments that could not be reliably identified were 

150 excluded from the sensitivity and specificity analysis.

213 214 Discussion 

215 Classi4RPE, which demonstrates good sensitivity and specificity for L and ML granules, can successfully segment 

216 and classify RPE FLIM images. However, generating a reliable ground ‑truth image proved challenging. The morphology of 

217 RPE granules is highly complex [1], making it difficult even for human observers to distinguish individual granules or separate 

218 those that are clustered. Classification was particularly difficult for M versus ML granules, as it depends not only on 

219 lifetime ‑image contrast but also on the neighboring area. For example, when an ML or M granule is surrounded by L granules, 

220 delineating the boundaries between them becomes difficult. Similar ambiguity arises when granules overlap or appear stacked. 

221 Consequently, the ground truth included only granules that could be confidently identified by visual inspection, and excluding 

222 uncertain granules from sensitivity and specificity calculations was justified. 

223 The highest sensitivity was achieved for detecting L granules, due to their distinct lifetime contrast, which applied as 

224 the first step in the classification pipeline (pre-classification). Nevertheless, achieving optimal segmentation of L granules 

225 remains challenging. Because segmentation was based on the intensity image, distinguishing true granules from sub ‑granular 

226 intensity variations was not always straightforward. This issue is particularly common for L granules, as previously reported 

227 by Bermond et al. (2020) [1]. Consequently, some L granules were over ‑segmented into multiple fragments—an outcome that 

228 cannot always be confirmed visually. These segmentation errors can lead to misclassification between L and ML granules, as 

229 illustrated in Figure 2: the yellow arrow indicates a large ML granule that was segmented into three parts, with the central 

230 segment classified as ML and the two outer segments as L. SIM imaging may also suggest the presence of multiple granules 

231 when viewed from a different imaging ‑modality perspective. The green arrow highlights a similar case for another granule. 

232 These segmentation uncertainties can propagate into misclassification, as shown in Figure 3. The yellow arrow marks 

233 a ML granule that was incorrectly segmented into two granules (one classified as L and the other as ML) despite both FLIM

243 Distinguishing melanosomes (M) from ML granules remains difficult, contributing to the low sensitivity and

249 Another factor influencing M/ML discrimination is the ratio threshold between central and peripheral lifetime values. 

250 Although this threshold was optimized based on the available datasets, it requires a compromise between M and ML 

251 classification. Given our greater interest in accurately identifying ML granules, the threshold was tuned to favor ML detection, 

252 even at the expense of losing some M granules. In addition, the sensitivity and specificity for M were neglected because of the 

253 difficulty classifying them with certainty.

262 Further improvements could focus on optimizing lifetime thresholding and refining the segmentation criteria. In 

263 addition, the Classi4RPE framework may benefit from integration with higher-resolution imaging modalities or extension to 

264 three-dimensional analysis. Such developments could enable more accurate segmentation of SIM images and provide a more 

265 robust comparison with FLIM ‑based performance. Overall, despite the inherent challenges associated with the complex nature 

266 of the data, the algorithm achieves high performance. Its strong sensitivity and specificity relative to annotated ground truth— 

267 and its ability to capture features beyond visual interpretation—highlight its reliability as a tool for RPE granule analysis and 

268 its potential as a practical and effective method for studying RPE cells in AMD research. 

269 Conclusion 

270 Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) induces significant alterations in RPE granules, necessitating further 

271 statistical investigation. Lifetime measurements provide important discriminatory information among these granules and can 

272 be used for their classification. However, visual classification remains challenging due to the difficulty of delineating granule 

273 boundaries, and some granules cannot be unambiguously assigned to a single type. 

274 In this study, we developed a computational framework that provides a robust and efficient approach for RPE 

275 analysis, enabling systematic statistical tracking at the level of individual granules. This approach also mitigates the 

276 limitations associated with the lack of a reliable ground truth. The proposed algorithm, Classi4RPE, segments RPE images 

277 acquired via two-photon excitation using a seeded watershed approach and classifies granules into L, M, and ML types based 

278 on synchronized lifetime data. 

279 High performance was achieved across seven datasets used to optimize the algorithm parameters, with almost 

280 complete coverage of the regions of interest and successful segmentation of most granules. This level of performance is 

281 difficult to achieve with conventional segmentation softwares, which often require extensive manual fine-tuning. Moreover, 

282 the algorithm demonstrated high sensitivity and specificity compared to manual ground truth annotations, highlighting its 

283 capability to exceed the limitations of human visual assessment in both segmentation and classification tasks. 

284 Despite these promising results, several areas for improvement remain. Further refinement of segmentation 

285 parameters could enhance classification accuracy. Additionally, extending the framework to support three-dimensional SIM
