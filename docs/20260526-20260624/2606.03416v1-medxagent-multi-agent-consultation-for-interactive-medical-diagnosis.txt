Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are increas-ingly used for health-related decision support. Yet most evaluations treat diagnosis as a single-shot task with complete information provided upfront, often as a multiple-choice selection. This diverges from clinical practice, where di-agnosis is interactive and open-ended, involv-ing sequential hypothesis refinement through targeted questioning. We address this gap. We build MeDxBench , a large-scale benchmark of 4,421 clinical cases across 20 specialties. We further propose MeDxAgent , a multi-agent consultation system for interactive diagnosis, and systematically study its prompt-, flow-and agent-level design choices. MeDxAgent 

achieves a 10.3% accuracy gain over the base-line on MeDxBench , closing 52.3% of the gap to a full-information oracle. We find that spe-cific design choices: collecting demographics first, passing summarized dialogue for diagno-sis, and feeding candidate diagnoses for tar-geted questioning, improve accuracy, mirror-ing how physicians reason, though their effect emerges fully only in combination. Code and dataset will be released upon publication. 

1 Introduction 

LLMs are increasingly used for real-world deci-sion support, with healthcare emerging as one of the most prominent application domains (Costa-Gomes et al., 2025, 2026). A substantial fraction of LLM usage involves health-related queries, with users relying on them to interpret symptoms and determine next steps. However, LLMs largely op-erate in a single-shot setting, producing diagnoses from incomplete symptom descriptions without it-erative information gathering. In contrast, clinical diagnosis is inherently interactive: physicians ask targeted questions, iteratively refine hypotheses, and perform differential diagnosis. Despite this, most benchmarks for medical di-agnosis evaluate LLMs in static task formulation. Models are provided complete case descriptions, often exam-style questions such as USMLE, and evaluated on their ability to predict the correct diagnosis (Jin et al., 2021; Singhal et al., 2023). Although LLMs perform strongly on such bench-marks, in some cases matching or exceeding physi-cians (Wang et al., 2025b; Singhal et al., 2025), the task lacks ecological validity. Recent work (Tu et al., 2025; Chiu et al., 2026; Almansoori et al., 2026) has begun exploring multi-turn doctor-patient interactions using LLM agents for disease diagnosis. However, several limita-tions remain. First , there is no standardized di-agnostic benchmark that combines diverse clinical cases with reliable diagnoses. Existing datasets are fragmented across formats (e.g., clinical vignettes, EHRs) and specialties (e.g., dermatology, cardiol-ogy), and mix diagnoses with unrelated tasks. Sec-ond , existing agentic approaches remain limited. Open systems often use simplistic paradigm of a single “doctor” agent that iteratively questions the patient until it determines enough information has been gathered for diagnosis (Chiu et al., 2026; Li et al., 2024; Liao et al., 2024), whereas the sophis-ticated multi-agent systems are closed-sourced and cannot be reproduced or compared (Tu et al., 2025; Nori et al., 2025). Third , several setups leak di-agnostic information and thereby weaken the task: For example, some initialize the doctor with part of the case (e.g., by passing the first sentence of the patient’s case description) (Li et al., 2024), some reduce diagnosis to multiple-choice selection (Li et al., 2024; Jia et al., 2025; Rose et al., 2025), some allow the patient agent to generate synthetic information beyond the case description using the ground truth diagnosis (Nori et al., 2025; Shang et al., 2025; Rose et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025a), and some convert case descriptions into structured fields that occasionally contain information reveal-ing diagnosis (Almansoori et al., 2026). prior work typically reports results from a single system configuration, leaving it unclear which de-sign choices are responsible for observed gains. We study this design space systematically. We first construct MeDxBench , a large-scale diagnosis benchmark of 4,421 clinical cases collected from five public sources, spanning 20 specialties and ranging from common to rare diseases. Our sim-ulated patient agent answers only from the case description, preventing diagnosis information leaks during conversation. We then propose MeDxAgent ,a multi-agent consultation system for interactive di-agnosis. We evaluate its prompt-, flow-, and agent-level design choices independently and in combi-nation, treating each choice as a hypothesis about the capabilities required for effective diagnosis. 

MeDxAgent improves diagnostic accuracy by 10.3% over the baseline on MeDxBench , and recov-ers 52.3% of the gap between the baseline and a full-information oracle. Our analysis reveals that three capabilities prove particularly important: col-lecting patient demographics first, summarizing doctor-patient dialogue into a clinical problem rep-resentation, and feeding diagnostic reasoning to guide subsequent questioning. Further, we reveal two broader findings. First, the effectiveness of an intervention depends strongly on when it is applied. For example, we introduce differential questioning, which directs the agent to ask questions that dis-tinguish among competing diagnoses. Applying this strategy from the second versus the tenth turn produces an 18.1% variation in diagnostic accuracy. Second, components that hurt performance in isola-tion can become beneficial in combination . Three of the four agents reduce accuracy individually, but the full system integrating all four outperforms every partial configuration. A standard incremen-tal ablation study would have incorrectly rejected these components.

LLMs now match or exceed expert-level perfor-mance on several such benchmarks (Wang et al., 2025b; Singhal et al., 2025). Multi-agent ap-proaches such as MDAgents (Kim et al., 2024), MDTeamGPT (Chen et al., 2025), Agent Hospi-tal (Li et al., 2025), and MedAgents (Tang et al., 2024) assign specialized roles to different agents, but still operate on fully observed cases. All these approaches sidesteps the iterative information-gathering central to clinical diagnosis. 

Interactive Diagnostic Systems. Recent work has begun exploring simulated doctor-patient interac-tions for diagnosis. Some approaches formulate the task as disease selection rather than open-ended diagnosis prediction. MediQ (Li et al., 2024) stud-ies iterative questioning to identify the correct di-agnosis from multiple choices; DDO (Jia et al., 2025) and MEDDxAgent (Rose et al., 2025) frame diagnosis as classification over a limited disease set. Other works focus on benchmarking LLMs by prompting them to act as clinicians conducting follow-up questioning, rather than designing multi-agent systems. CRAFT-MD (Johri et al., 2025) introduces a conversational evaluation framework spanning multiple specialties; VivaBench (Chiu et al., 2026) studies cognitive failure modes in LLM diagnosis, and identifies anchoring bias. Most re-cently, agentic approaches have been developed for open-ended conversational diagnosis. AMIE (Tu et al., 2025), optimized for diagnostic reasoning and conversations, demonstrated physician-level performance on 149 simulated primary-care consul-tations with trained human actors; MAI-DxO (Nori et al., 2025) orchestrated a panel of physician-persona agents to diagnose 304 complex NEJM cases at physician-level accuracy. However, both systems are closed-source and their evaluation pro-tocols are not reproducible. Several open sys-tems have also been proposed, such as Agent-Clinic (Schmidgall et al., 2025), AI Hospital (Fan et al., 2025) and MedAgentSim (Almansoori et al., 2026). However, these works evaluate either a sin-gle doctor agent configuration or have relatively small benchmarks, limiting systematic analysis of design choices (Table 1). In contrast, we systemat-ically study how prompt design, interaction flow, and agent composition affect multi-turn diagnostic performance across a large benchmark. 

3 MeDxBench Benchmark Dataset 

MeDxBench is a large-scale benchmark for medi-cal diagnosis containing 4,421 cases spanning 20 clinical specialties (Table 6). Each case includes a unique identifier, case description, source dataset, specialty, and ground-truth diagnosis label(s). 

Sources. We define a diagnostic case as a patient case description paired with a ground-truth diagno-sis. We build MeDxBench by aggregating and stan-dardizing cases from five public datasets that meet this criterion: CRAFT-MD (Johri et al., 2025) ( 200 

cases), DiagnosisArena (Zhu et al., 2025) ( 897 ), MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022) ( 847 ), MedQA (Jin et al., 2021) ( 1,498 ), and PubMed cases curated by VivaBench (Chiu et al., 2026) ( 988 ). 

Statistics. The dataset spans diverse case for-mats, difficulties, and specialties. Case formats in-clude exam-style questions (MedQA, MedMCQA), free-text clinical vignettes (CRAFT-MD), and peer-reviewed case reports (DiagnosisArena, PubMed). Its 4,113 unique disease labels comprise 655 com-mon (high prevalence, > 1 in 100 globally), 1,098 

uncommon (moderate prevalence, 1 in 100 to 1 in 

2,000 ) and 2,360 rare ( < 1 in 2,000 ) conditions. It spans 20 specialties, with the largest propor-tions from Infectious Diseases ( 11 .1% ), Neurology (9.7% ) and Emergency Medicine ( 8.9% ). Case descriptions range from 212 to 9,848 characters (mean 1,785 ± 1,955 ), and 546 cases have more than one ground-truth diagnosis labels. 

Construction. From the five datasets, we keep only diagnostic cases with sufficient clinical de-tail. We remove cases focused on medical knowl-edge, treatment recommendations, or other non-diagnostic tasks. We keep only descriptions longer than 200 characters. Because our work studies text-only diagnosis, we exclude cases requiring im-age/video interpretation, while retaining those con-taining textual imaging reports. We also remove 3duplicate cases appearing across datasets. To prevent diagnosis leakage , we redact explicit mentions of the ground-truth diagnosis from each case description. We manually verify that redaction does not remove clinically relevant evidence. 

4 Simulation Framework 

Our framework simulates multi-turn doctor-patient conversations using three agents: (i) a Patient agent that answers from a case description, (ii) a Doc-tor agent that conducts the consultation and (iii) a Judge agent that evaluates the final diagnosis. All agents are implemented through prompt engineer-ing; full prompts are provided in Appendix C. The simplest configuration serves as our baseline . Its Doctor agent comprise of two components: a Ques-tion agent that interacts with the patient and a Diag-nosis agent that predicts candidate diagnoses after each turn. Our full system, MeDxAgent , adds a summarizer, specialist ensemble, knowledge-graph module, and evidence-gap finder (Section 5). At initialization, the Patient receives the case de-scription and the Judge receives the ground-truth diagnosis. The Doctor begins with a standard open-ing question (“What brings you in today?”), to which the Patient responds with the primary con-cern. At each subsequent turn, the Doctor asks a question conditioned on the conversation history, and the Patient responds strictly from the case de-scription, returning “I don’t know” if the requested information is not present. After each turn, the Doc-tor outputs three candidate diagnoses, each with a short rationale and a confidence score. The inter-action continues until either a diagnosis reaches a 95% confidence threshold, selected on a 300-case validation split, or a 20-turn limit is reached (fol-lowing (Li et al., 2024)). In the latter case, the highest-confidence diagnosis from the last turn is selected. The final prediction is then evaluated by the Judge. All experiments use temperature = 0 .

Patient Agent . The Patient answers strictly from the case description, without fabricating or inferring unstated details, responding with “I don’t know” otherwise. This prevents diagnosis leakage, since the Patient has access only to the case de-scription and not the ground-truth label. Cases are typically written retrospectively (e.g., as clinical notes), so we instruct the agent to respond as if currently experiencing the condition, in the first person, and in plain language. 

Doctor Agent. The baseline Doctor has two components, both operating at each turn. (1) Ques-tion Agent generates the next query and a brief rationale, from the conversation history. We in-struct it to ask simple, atomic questions covering key clinical dimensions (including demographics, symptoms, medical and family history, physical examination findings, and test results), to avoid repeating questions, to prioritize diagnostically in-formative questions, and to shift its line of inquiry when the Patient answers "I don’t know." (2) Di-agnosis Agent produces three candidate diagnoses from the conversation history, each with a short jus-tification and self-reported confidence score. The agent is not conditioned on its own prior predic-tions; Section 5 studies that choice directly. 

Judge Agent . The Judge uses LLM-based se-mantic matching to determine whether the pre-dicted and ground-truth diagnosis refer to the same condition. A prediction is considered correct if it is (i) an exact match, (ii) a recognized synonym or paraphrase (e.g., high BP vs. hypertension), or (iii) a more specific subtype of the ground-truth (e.g., malignant melanoma for melanoma). Predictions more general than the ground-truth are considered incorrect. For multi-label cases, a match with any ground-truth label counts as correct, since multiple diagnoses can explain the same presentation. 

5 MeDxAgent System 

We study how design choices affect multi-turn diagnosis. We treat each design choice as a hy-pothesis and group them by where they intervene: (1) Prompt-based choices modify the instructions provided to the Question agent, (2) Flow-based 

choices change the interaction flow, and (3) Agent-based choices introduce additional agents for sum-marization, reasoning, or knowledge grounding. We evaluate each choice incrementally(Table 2). The Patient and Judge agents remain fixed across all variants, ensuring that performance differences arise only from the design choice under study. We use clinician analogies to motivate these design choices, not to claim physician-like reasoning. 

5.1 Prompt-Based Variations 

We evaluate two prompt modifications to the base-line, as well as their combination. 

Turn Awareness. We provide the Question agent with the remaining question budget and turn-specific guidance: early turns (1-5) focus on chief complaints and high-yield screening questions, 4middle turns (6-15) gather objective evidence and differentiate symptoms, and late turns (16-20) pri-oritize the most discriminative questions. Because early questions shape the utility of later follow-ups, question ordering compounds over the consultation. We test whether turn budget awareness helps the agent plan and allocate its questions better. 

Demographics First. We require the Question agent to collect age and sex in the first turn. This mirrors clinical practice, where demographic in-formation helps anchor the differential diagnosis (Chang et al., 1998). In the baseline, the agent is encouraged, but not required, to ask about demo-graphics. We test whether fixing demographics at the start orients the diagnostic process by ruling out age- and sex-dependent implausible candidates. 

Combined Prompt. We combine both modi-fications; apply turn awareness at every step and require demographic collection in the first turn. 

5.2 Flow-Based Variations 

Building on the best prompt variant (Table 2), Com-bined Prompt , we study flow-based modifications. 

No Early Stopping. The baseline ends a consul-tation once the Diagnosis agent reports ≥ 95% con-fidence. Here, we remove this and run all consul-tations for the full 20 turns, and select the highest-confidence diagnosis from the final turn. We test whether gathering more evidence before commit-ting improves diagnostic accuracy. 

Differential Questioning. In the baseline, the next question is generated independently of the current diagnosis predictions. Here, the Ques-tion agent additionally receives the candidate diag-noses, confidence scores, and supporting reasoning, and is instructed to ask questions that differenti-ate competing hypotheses. This is motivated by hypothetico-deductive reasoning, in which physi-cians form hypotheses early and gather evidence to confirm or reject them (Elstein et al., 1978). This strategy has a known failure mode: conditioning too early on weak hypotheses can lead to anchor-ing (Graber et al., 2005; Chiu et al., 2026). We therefore activate differential questioning only af-ter a fixed turn threshold, testing switch points at turns 2, 5, and 10. Activating at turn 2 enables it almost from the start, since the first turn collects demographics; switching at later turns allow more general evidence to accumulate before conditioning future questions on diagnostic hypotheses. 

Combined Flow. We combine no early stopping with differential questioning activated at turn 10. 

5.3 Agent-Based Variations 

Building on the best Flow variant, Differential Questioning (Turn 10) , we introduce four agents. 

Summarizer. Instead of passing the raw con-versation to the Diagnosis agent, a Summarizer converts it into a structured clinical representation. A physician distills an encounter into a concise problem representation that organizes findings and activates relevant diagnosis (Bowen, 2006). Addi-tionally, LLMs are trained primarily on case-style medical text (e.g., clinical notes, USMLE-style vi-gnettes) rather than conversational transcripts, mak-ing summarized representations better aligned with their training data. We test two formats: (a) a free-form paragraph-style clinical narrative, and (b) a structured SOAP-style schema (Wong et al., 2025) with fields such as symptoms_present ,

medical_history , and test_results .

Specialist Ensemble. We use an ensemble of ten physician agents: nine specialists (Cardiology, Pulmonology, Gastroenterology, Neurology, En-docrinology, Nephrology, Rheumatology, Haema-tology, and Dermatology) and one general physi-cian. Each agent reviews the conversation and predicts its top three diagnoses with confidence scores and reasoning, producing a diverse candi-date pool. A separate Selector-SE agent, designed as a senior physician, selects the final top three di-agnosis by weighting specialists according to their relevance to the current case rather than by major-ity voting. This follows multidisciplinary clinical practice, where diverse specialists contribute to a single case. We test whether such diversity surfaces hypotheses missed by a single generalist agent. 

Knowledge Graph. Structured medical knowl-edge sources can surface relevant candidates and improve the factual grounding of predictions (Gao et al., 2025). We therefore ground candidate diag-noses generation in the Unified Biomedical Knowl-edge Graph (UBKG) (Stear et al., 2024). At each turn, we query UBKG using symptoms revealed by the Patient and aggregate the diseases it re-turns. Diseases are scored based on the number of matched symptoms, and the top three are passed to the Selector-KG agent. As UBKG does not in-corporate clinical evidence such as test results, the base Diagnosis agent also generates its own top three predictions using the conversation history. The Selector-KG receives both sets and selects a final top three based on consistency with the conver-sation. We test whether knowledge graph ground-5ing contributes complementary hypotheses that the LLM overlooks. 

Evidence Gap. Clinicians gather information selectively, directing questions toward the evidence that would most change the differential diagno-sis (Bowen, 2006). The Evidence Gap agent fol-lows this principle, identifying information still needed to support or reject a diagnosis. It identi-fies: (i) diagnosis-specific gaps that help differen-tiate current candidate diagnoses, and (ii) general gaps covering broadly informative clinical details (such as vitals, lab results). The agent outputs up to three gaps per type, expressed as short, actionable statements, ranked by estimated diagnostic utility. Before turn 10, when candidate diagnoses remain unused by the Question agent, it reports only gen-eral gaps using the dialog history. From turn 10, when Differential Questioning is active, it also re-ceives the top three candidates and reports both gap types. The Question agent is prompted to priori-tize these gaps when forming the next query. We test whether explicitly modeling missing evidence improves information gathering. 

Combined Agents: MeDxAgent . It integrates all four agents (Figure 1). The Summarizer runs at every turn, converting dialogue into a structured clinical representation. During early turns (1-9), the Question agent gathers information broadly, guided only by general evidence gaps. From turn 10, the system enters a differential diagnosis phase. The Knowledge Graph retrieves symptom-matched diseases, the Specialist Ensemble proposes addi-tional specialty-specific hypotheses, and the Selec-tor consolidates these candidates into a final top three. The Evidence Gap agent now reports both general and diagnosis-specific gaps, enabling the Question agent to ask question based on current hypotheses and missing evidence. 

6 Results 

Using GPT-4o as the underlying model, MeDxA-gent achieves 57.4% average diagnostic accuracy on MeDxBench , 10.3 points above the baseline (47.1%) (Table 2). Performance improves mono-tonically across the prompt-, flow-, and agent-level additions. Gains hold across all five constituent datasets, although absolute accuracy varies: from 27.4% on DiagnosisArena to 66.6% on PubMed. The full information Oracle, which receives the complete case upfront, reaches only 66.8%, indi-cating that the task is far from solved even with perfect information. Against this ceiling, MeDx-Agent closes 52.3% of the baseline-to-Oracle gap. The remaining 9.4-point gap is likely due to these reasons: degradation of LLM performance in multi-turn settings (Laban et al., 2026), incomplete infor-mation due to frequent “I don’t know” responses from the Patient agent, and possible training-data contamination suggested by PubMed’s near-ceiling Oracle accuracy (92.0%). 

Different variants contribute differently. 

Combined Prompt reaches 51.7%, improving over the baseline by 4.6 points through two changes to the Question agent: collecting demographics in the first turn and conditioning on the remain-ing turn budget. Combined Flow reaches 52.8%, driven primarily by differential questioning. Treat-ing differential questioning as a timed interven-tion rather than an always-on capability reveals strong sensitivity. Activating it at turn 2 reduces accuracy to 34.7%, 12.4 points below the base-line. Delaying activation to turn 5 recovers most of the loss (50.4%) and turn 10 achieves 52.8%, yielding an 18.1-point swing from changing only the switch point. Removing early stopping does not improve performance over Combined Prompt (51.6% vs. 51.7%), which suggests that the Diag-nosis agent’s confidence-based stopping criterion is well-calibrated: consultations end early (mean 16.3 turns) once sufficient evidence is gathered. 

The agent layer shows strong compositional effects. The agent layer provides the largest single gain, from 52.8% at Differential Questioning (Turn 10) to 57.4% with all four agents (+4.6 points). Individually, only the Summarizer improves per-formance, in both formats (+2.1 paragraph, +1.8 structured). The other three agents each reduce accuracy by 1-2 points when added in isolation. Even though the sum of the four individual gains is negative, the full system outperforms every par-tial configuration. Leave-one-out ablations further confirm this dependence: removing any agent from 

MeDxAgent reduces accuracy below the full system (Table 5), indicating that no agent is redundant. The largest accuracy drop occurs when removing the (Summarizer) agent, which contributed the most. 

Cross-model transfer. The design choices de-veloped on GPT-4o transfer to other model fam-ilies, Grok-4.1 and DeepSeek-V3.2, without re-tuning prompts or thresholds (Table 3). Comparison with prior work. We compare

7 Discussion 

What worked and why. Three design choices account for most of the gains, and each aligns with established principles of clinical reasoning. First, collecting demographics first orient the dif-ferential diagnosis because age and sex strongly shape disease priors. For instance, in 39 pediatric cases, demographics were the key differentiating factor in diagnosis: the baseline defaulted to adult presentations and missed age-specific conditions, whereas the demographics-first variant correctly 7identified them. This matches prior reports of de-mographic biases in medical LLMs (Rawat et al., 2024) and shows a single-line prompt intervention can help mitigate them in an agentic system. Sec-ond, Differential Questioning helps, but only after sufficiently strong hypotheses have formed. This mirrors hypothetico-deductive pattern of clinical reasoning, in which physicians form hypotheses and gather evidence to confirm or rule them out (El-stein et al., 1978), and its associated failure mode, anchoring, where weak early hypotheses bias sub-sequent reasoning (Graber et al., 2005). Our ex-periments show that the same instruction and in-teraction flow can improve or reduce performance depending solely on when it is activated. Third, the Summarizer improves accuracy by changing how evidence is represented rather than what evidence is available. Clinicians refer to this as problem representation, and prior work links higher-quality representations to more accurate diagnosis (Bowen, 2006). Also, summarization may help because structured clinical summaries more closely resem-ble the case-style medical text on which LLMs are predominantly trained. 

Why three agents fail in isolation. The remain-ing three agents lower accuracy individually, each for a different reason. The Specialist Ensemble and Knowledge Graph (KG) variants are prone to premature termination (Table 7). In the Specialist Ensemble, repeated predictions across specialists inflate aggregate confidence during ensembling. In the KG variant, the Selector-KG overweights KG-derived candidates. These causes early stopping before sufficient evidence is gathered, reducing ac-curacy. The Evidence Gap agent appears to fail due to confirmation bias. Although it extracts more information, as reflected by a sharp reduction in “I don’t know” answers relative to the baseline (Fig-ure 5), it tends to direct questions toward confirm-ing the leading hypothesis. When that hypothesis is incorrect, the system gathers increasingly de-tailed evidence for the wrong disease, lowering performance. Despite this, each agent contributes positively in MeDxAgent . Leave-one-out ablations (Table 5) show that removing any agent from the full system lowers accuracy. We do not claim that the combined system fully resolves these issues; however, we provide empirical evidence for why the combined setup leads to improved accuracy. 

Components work in combination. The strong compositional effects arise because the modules are designed to operate jointly. The Summarizer produces a structured representation, which helps the Knowledge Graph and Specialist Ensemble gen-erate ‘diverse’ diagnostic candidates. These can-didates enable Evidence Gap to identify missing evidence more reliably, which in turn helps the Question agent to ask more targeted questions and reduce "I don’t know" responses. As discussed, individually, the Knowledge Graph and Special-ist Ensemble tend to be overconfident, leading to premature termination. When combined, however, their predictions are balanced, reflected in lower early stopping rates (Table 7). Similarly, the Ev-idence Gap agent alone is prone to confirmation bias, but benefits from the more diverse candidate set produced by the combined system, reducing anchoring effects. Overall, each agent in isolation perturbs a system not built to absorb it, often reduc-ing accuracy. Together, the agents become comple-mentary, and removing any one lowers accuracy. Prior multi-agent medical systems typically report aggregate gains over single-agent baselines without isolating individual agent contributions (Nori et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024), leaving such compositional effects unexplored. We high-light this phenomenon empirically, though we do not claim it generalizes to all multi-agent systems. 

Real-world deployment. Despite promising performance, MeDxAgent should be viewed strictly as a decision-support tool for clinicians, not a re-placement for medical professionals. It is not suit-able for autonomous use by non-medical person-nel. Incorrect or overconfident diagnoses could de-lay treatment, trigger unnecessary interventions, or cause serious patient harm, particularly in high-risk clinical settings. Moreover, conversational diagnos-tic systems may inherit biases (e.g., demography-related) and reasoning failures from their underly-ing LLMs. In practice, such systems may be most appropriate for assisting physicians with differen-tial diagnosis, surfacing alternative hypotheses, or double-checking clinical decisions. 

8 Conclusion 

We present MeDxBench , a benchmark of 4,421 clin-ical cases across 20 specialities for open-ended in-teractive diagnosis and MeDxAgent , a multi-agent system built from a systematic study of prompt-, flow- and agent-level design choices. Beyond these, our contribution is methodological: we isolate the impact of individual design decisions and reveal interaction effects. 89 Limitations 

First, our evaluation is limited to text-only diag-nosis, while real-world clinical diagnosis often de-pends on multimodal evidence such as imaging. Second, the Patient agent answers strictly from the case description, which prevents diagnosis leakage, but reduces the natural variability and ecological va-lidity of real patient interactions, as patients rarely respond with repeated "I don’t know" responses. Finally, although we systematically study several design choices, a broader sweep over prompts, in-teraction flows, and agent configurations would strengthen the empirical conclusions; though such sweeps are computationally expensive in multi-turn agentic settings. Future work can address these lim-itations and evaluate whether the design choices that help in simulation also transfer to supervised clinical use, with multimodal inputs, and learned questioning policies beyond prompt engineering.

A.1 Accuracy by Specialty 

Figure 2 reports diagnostic accuracy across 20 spe-cialties for all variants, and Figure 3 shows the con-fusion matrix between ground-truth and predicted specialties. MeDxAgent is the best performing variant in 15 of 20 specialties, with its highest ac-curacies in Endocrinology (68.7%), Orthopaedics (68.0%), and Obstetrics & Gynaecology (65.3%). It improves over the baseline in all specialties ex-cept Emergency Medicine, where its predictions are frequently shifted towards Neurology and Pul-monology. The confusion matrix yields a specialty-match accuracy (i.e., the predicted diagnosis belongs to the correct specialty) of 70.4%. Most specialty confusions are clinically adjacent. Ophthalmology (86.8%), Obstetrics & Gynaecology (84.0%), Neu-rology (83.7%), and Dermatology (83.0%) achieve the highest specialty-match accuracy, indicating that these specialties have distinctive clinical pre-sentations that our system rarely confuses with other domains. In contrast, Rheumatology errors are scattered across Cardiology, Neurology and Nephrology, consistent with autoimmune diseases presenting through the organ systems they affect. In such cases, our system predicts an organ-specific diagnosis rather than the underlying systemic dis-order. 

A.2 Accuracy by Disease Prevalence 

Figure 4 reports accuracy across three disease prevalence tiers. For cases with multiple ground-truth diagnoses, evaluation uses the most prevalent diagnosis among the labels. MeDxAgent ’s gains over the baseline is inversely correlated with dis-ease prevalence: +4.8 points on common diseases (56.3% to 61.1%), +9.1 points on uncommon dis-eases (55.4% to 64.5%), and +11.9 points on rare diseases (37.1% to 49.0%). Among individual com-ponents, the Summarizer (paragraph format) per-forms best on rare cases (45.7% against 42.1% for the best flow variant), consistent with summariza-tion helping most when findings are sparse and scattered across the conversation, as they often are in rare presentations.

A.4 Accuracy of the Patient Agent 

We evaluate the Patient agent using an LLM-as-a-judge over all responses generated by MeDxA-gent , totaling 84,739 question-answer pairs across 4,421 cases. The Patient agent achieves an overall response accuracy of 97.2%. Among the 2,387 in-accurate responses (2.8%), we identify four error types: withholding (1,513 responses; 63.4% of er-rors), where the Patient omits information present in the case description, typically failing to mention a detail when asked a broad question; fabrication 

(457; 19.1%), where the Patient introduces clinical details not present in the case; contradiction (368; 15.4%), where the response conflicts with informa-tion in the case; and oversharing (48; 2.0%), where the Patient provides information not solicited by the doctor’s question. Overall, these results suggest that the Patient agent remains highly faithful to the underlying case descriptions.

C.3 Doctor: Diagnosis Agents 

After each dialog turn, a Diagnosis Agent pro-duces ranked set of top-3 differential diagnosis list with supporting reasoning and calibrated con-fidence scores. Several versions of this agent are used depending on the system configuration. In the Specialist-Ensemble variant, 9 Specialist agents 

(fields Cardiology, Pulmonology, Gastroenterology, Neurology, Endocrinology, Nephrology, Rheuma-tology, Hematology, Dermatology) along with one generalist Diagnosis Agent independently produce top-3 differentials from the clinical information collected so far; a Selector-SE Agent then consoli-dates these 30 candidate predictions into a single top-3 differential set with reasoning and confidence scores, weighting each specialist’s opinion by its relevance to the case. In the Knowledge-Base vari-ant, the top-3 diagnoses derived from the UBKG-retrieved candidate diseases are combined with the predictions of the general Diagnosis Agent using a Selector-KG Agent , which produces a final top-3 differential set with reasoning and confidence scores. In the MeDxAgent ariant, the output of the 

Selector-SE agent and top-3 UBKG candidates is filtered down to three diagnosis using the Selector 

agent.

C.5 Evidence Gap Agent 

The Evidence Gap agent identifies clinically infor-mative missing evidence from the current case state. In early turns, it highlights general missing infor-mation (e.g., vitals, labs, history details), while after differential questioning begins, it additionally identifies diagnosis-specific gaps that would help distinguish among the current top candidate diag-noses. These gaps are then used by the Evidence-Guided question agents (EG-QA and EG-DQA, Section C.2) to guide subsequent question selec-tion. 

Evidence Gap Agent Prompt 

Role. You are a clinical evidence analyst identi-fying missing diagnostic information. 

Instruction. Given the current clinical summary and top-3 differential diagnoses, produce an evidence-gap report. 

Gap types. 

• General gaps: missing baseline informa-tion useful for any diagnostic workup, such as demographics, vitals, symptom timeline, medical history, medications, family history, exposures, and basic tests. 

• Diagnosis-specific gaps: missing evi-dence required to confirm or rule out each candidate diagnosis. 

Rules. 

• Focus on actionable information that can be obtained from the patient or standard tests. 

• Prioritize gaps that would most strongly shift confidence between competing diag-noses. 

• Include red-flag gaps when missing infor-mation could indicate serious disease. 

• Provide concise, clinically useful missing-evidence statements.

• Clinical summary: {summary} 

• Current top-3 differential diagnoses: {pre-dictions}

C.6 Judge Agent 

The Judge evaluates the final predicted diagno-sis against the ground-truth label using seman-tic matching, accepting exact matches, synonyms, paraphrases and more specific subtypes of the ground-truth diagnosis (not vice versa), while re-jecting different or even near-misses across distinct conditions (e.g., Type 1 vs. Type 2 diabetes). 

Judge Agent Prompt 

Role. You are a medical terminology expert com-paring disease names. 

Instruction. Determine whether the predicted disease and the ground-truth disease refer to the same medical condition. 

Rules. 

• Treat medical synonyms, abbreviations, and common-name variants as equivalent. 

• Treat a predicted disease as correct if it is a more specific subtype of the ground truth. 

• Be strict when clinically distinct subtypes are involved; they should not be treated as the same condition, e.g., Type 1 vs. Type 2 diabetes. 

• Return false when the diseases are related but not equivalent. 

Input. 

• Predicted disease: {predicted} 

• Ground-truth disease: {ground_truth} 

Output format.
