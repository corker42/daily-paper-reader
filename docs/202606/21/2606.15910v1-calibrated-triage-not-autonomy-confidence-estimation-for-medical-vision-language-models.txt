Abstract —A vision-language model can answer a question about a medical image, a chest radiograph or a pathology slide, fluently and confidently while barely using the image, leaning instead on language priors. In medicine this is the failure that matters most, because the answer looks trustworthy and is not, and the only protection is a confidence score reliable enough to tell the system when to abstain. We therefore ask a deployment question rather than an accuracy one: how much medical imaging work a vision-language model can safely handle on its own, and which confidence signal makes that possible. We evaluate seven confidence estimators, from prompt-based self-reports to trained internal probes, across five open-weight LVLMs and three medical visual-question-answering datasets spanning broad clinical imaging, radiology, and pathology, with every probe trained only on natural images and applied to medicine without adaptation. Recast as bounded selective predic-tion, where a case is automated only when its confidence clears a threshold and the rest are deferred, the comparison yields a consistent and cautionary picture. The standard metrics are poor guides: discrimination barely separates the methods, and the weak average calibration of a cheap self-report is largely removed by an off-domain temperature fit that leaves its deployable yield unchanged. What distinguishes a usable estimator is the high-confidence region a clinician would act on, where the weakest baselines are confidently wrong on 41 to 45 percent of their errors against 1 to 4 percent for the best trained probe, and no estimator is reliably best across the three domains or the five models. How much can be safely handed off is governed at two levels: base-model competence on a domain sets a ceiling, so a well-calibrated score recovers roughly a third of radiology cases at a 20 percent error tolerance but almost none of pathology, and the confidence layer then decides how much of that ceiling is safely reachable. The usable role today is calibrated triage, not autonomy: automate only the cases a calibrated score marks as safe and route the rest to a clinician. How confidence is measured and aggregated, more than which model produces it, decides how much of the work can be handed off. We release all model outputs, correctness judgments, and confidence scores, with code. 

Index Terms —confidence estimation, calibration, selective pre-diction, vision-language models, medical visual question answer-ing, trustworthy clinical AI 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Medical image interpretation needs trustworthy confi-dence, not only accuracy. Large vision-language models (LVLMs) are increasingly proposed to assist medical image

interpretation and to ease the documentation and review bur-den that drives clinician workload [1], [2]. An LVLM prepends visual tokens to a language backbone, but architectural access to the image does not mean the answer depends on it: attention heads can attend far more strongly to text than to visual tokens, and answer distributions are often nearly unchanged when the image is replaced by a blank input [3], [4]. A model can therefore produce a fluent and confident answer to a question about a radiograph or a pathology slide while barely using the image, drawing instead on language priors. The operative clinical question is consequently not whether a model is sometimes right, but whether its confidence can indicate when an answer should be trusted, reviewed, or withheld. 

Clinical confidence is unreliable, and the visual case is the sharpest one. Clinical language and vision-language models are systematically overconfident, and the failure has a consistent shape. A model tends to report nearly the same confidence whether its answer is right or wrong, so the score itself gives a clinician little to act on [5], [6], and this gap is widest on the highest-risk cases, exactly where a trustworthy signal is needed most [7]. Nor is it a surface artifact that better prompting removes: rewording the question leaves the overconfidence in place [8], which means the problem is built into how these models represent their own certainty rather than into how they are asked. That is what makes adoption outpacing trust a real risk and not mere caution, as hospitals put these models into use faster than the evidence shows they can be relied on [9], [10]. The multimodal setting is the sharpest form of this structural gap, because a model can be fluent, confident, and even correct on an answer it produced without using the image at all [4]: not reasoning incorrectly about the scan but bypassing it, which is exactly the output a clinician must not trust, an answer about an image the model did not, behaviorally, look at. 

Existing estimators are tuned for the wrong objective and untested on medical imaging. Confidence estimators are built and tuned for general-domain ranking under roughly uniform error cost, but the clinical target is different: not to rank cases well on average, but to draw a principled line between what can be automated and what must be escalated, while holding error on the automated side below a tolerated level [11]. Meeting that target takes more than accuracy. It takes calibrated probabilities a clinician can act on at the level

of a single patient [12], together with the ability to abstain when the model is uncertain [11]. No prior work, to our knowledge, tests confidence estimators against this objective on medical imaging under the conditions a clinical site actually faces, where a signal learned on ordinary images must hold on scans it has never seen. We study open-weight LVLMs for the same clinical reason: hospitals commonly require private, on-premises inference [13], [14], and the internal-state estimators we examine read model internals that closed APIs keep hidden. 

This study. We evaluate seven confidence estimators, spanning prompt-based self-reports and trained internal probes, across five open-weight LVLMs and three medical visual-question-answering datasets that span broad clinical imaging, radiology and anatomy, and pathology. Every trainable probe is trained only on general-domain natural images and applied to each medical dataset without adaptation, so the medical results are a test of transfer rather than in-domain fitting. We frame the comparison as bounded selective prediction: a case is automated only when its confidence clears a threshold, and the rest deferred to a clinician. The picture is consistent and cautionary. The standard metrics are poor guides to which estimator to trust, no single estimator is best across domains or models, and how much can be safely automated is set first by the model’s competence on a domain and only then by the confidence it carries. 

Contributions. This work makes three contributions. The first is the study itself: to our knowledge the first systematic out-of-distribution comparison of confidence estimation for medical LVLMs, putting seven estimators across five open-weight models and three medical VQA datasets through one deployment-oriented protocol. The second is a deployment account of what those estimators buy, recasting confidence estimation as bounded selective prediction and finding that safe automation is governed at two levels: base-model competence fixes a ceiling that falls from radiology to pathology and replicates inside a single benchmark, and the confidence layer then decides how much of it a clinic can safely reach. The third is the diagnostic lesson for why the usual evaluation misleads: triage depends on the high-confidence region aclinician would act on, not on aggregate ranking or average calibration, and only the grounding-aware estimator lowers its confidence when the model answers without using the image. II. R ELATED WORK 

What a confidence score must do. A confidence estimator attaches to each prediction a score meant to express how likely that prediction is to be correct. For triage, two properties matter and they are distinct. A score is calibrated when its value matches empirical accuracy, so that answers assigned 0.8 confidence are correct about 80 percent of the time; it is dis-criminative when higher scores are reliably more often correct than lower ones, regardless of the absolute scale. Calibration is what lets a fixed threshold map to a controlled error rate, the operation a deferral policy performs, while discrimination only orders cases. A score can have one property without the other, and this study asks whether either, learned on ordinary images, still holds on medical scans the probe has never seen. 

Confidence estimation for language and vision-language models. Methods fall into families distinguished by where the signal is read. Prompt-based methods elicit a verbal or token-level self-assessment (P(True) [15], Self-Probing [16], Prompt Ensembles [17]); because they ask the model to introspect on the same computation that produced its answer, their reliability rests on introspective capacity, which is poor for clinical rea-soning [8]. Internal-state probes bypass introspection and train a lightweight classifier on hidden activations: SAPLMA [18] reads the post-answer state, and InternalInspector [19] pools attention, feed-forward, and residual states through a learned encoder. Internal-stability methods instead read how a repre-sentation responds to controlled perturbation (CCPS [20]). All were tuned for general-domain discrimination under roughly uniform error cost, and prior work notes a tension between optimizing calibration and discrimination jointly [21]. We evaluate one or more methods from each family and ask a different, clinically motivated question: moved to medical imaging, how much workload can these estimators safely automate at a fixed error budget? 

Calibration, grounding, and selective deferral in medical AI. Prepending visual tokens to a language backbone does not guarantee that the image drives the answer [3], and hidden-state probes degrade when the visual signal is degraded, indicating that they read the quality of the visual signal rather than whether any visual signal was used at all [4]. LVLMs are persistently miscalibrated, with verbalized confidence track-ing correctness poorly [22], a problem that standard post-hoc calibration such as temperature scaling [23] does not fully resolve, particularly under distribution shift. One recent method, BICR [24], targets this grounding failure directly by training on the contrast between real-image and blank-image representations, and is among the estimators we evaluate. On the clinical side, calibrated probability estimates are what en-able patient-level decision-making [12], and selective deferral, abstaining when uncertain rather than maximizing aggregate accuracy, is argued to be the prerequisite for safe medical machine learning [11]. What is missing is a systematic evalu-ation of confidence estimators on medical imaging against this deployment objective and under realistic out-of-distribution transfer, which is the gap this study addresses. III. S TUDY DESIGN 

Data and protocol. Every trainable confidence estimator is trained and validated only on general-domain GQA [25] (20,000 train, 5,000 validation) and evaluated on three medical visual-question-answering datasets without any adaptation, so all medical sets are unseen during training for all methods. The three are chosen to span distinct imaging types and distinct distances from the natural-image training data. GMAI-MMBench [26] is a large-scale benchmark covering radiology, pathology, dermatology, ophthalmology, endoscopy, and sur-gical imaging across more than forty clinical VQA tasks, with disease diagnosis dominant; we use its full answer-bearing test set, and for the fine-grained analysis its clinical metadata axes (clinical task, department, imaging modality, perceptual granularity). SLAKE [27] is a radiology-and-anatomy bench-mark of CT, MRI, and X-ray with structured questions about organs, modalities, and findings, carrying anatomical-region metadata that supports a within-dataset gradient analysis. PATH-VQA [28] is a pathology benchmark of microscopy and histopathology images, the domain furthest from natural images, whose only subgroupings are grammatical rather than anatomical. Running one protocol across the three lets us ask whether conclusions about confidence estimation repli-cate across medical imaging or are domain-specific, and the differences between the datasets prove as informative as the within-dataset rankings. 

Models. We evaluate five open-weight, instruction-tuned LVLMs spanning 4.5B to 27B active parameters and three vision-encoder lineages: Qwen3-VL-8B, LLaVA-NeXT-13B, InternVL3.5-14B, DeepSeek-VL2, and Gemma-3-27B. All run under identical generation conditions (greedy decoding, 64 new tokens). Base VQA accuracy spans a wide, clinically realistic range and, crucially for the deployment analysis, differs systematically across the three domains: pooled over the five LVLMs it is 60.9% on SLAKE, 47.2% on GMAI-MMBench, and 39.5% on PATH-VQA, ordering the domains by their distance from the natural-image training distribution. In this regime no single LVLM is reliable on its own, so how much can be safely delegated depends jointly on the model’s competence on a domain and on the confidence layer that decides which answers to trust. 

Correctness labels. Free-form medical answers cannot be graded by string match: “the cardiac silhouette is enlarged” is correct against a gold answer of “heart,” but exact-match rules cannot accurately score it so. We therefore assign every answer a binary correctness label y ∈ { 0, 1} (1 correct, 0 incorrect) with a single gpt-5-mini judge that sees the image, the question, the gold answer, and the generated response, and decides whether the response is semantically equivalent to the gold answer. Applying one judge to every dataset and model is a deliberate control, not a convenience: it ensures that differences in measured confidence quality reflect model behavior rather than grading rules that vary across datasets. This protocol is well established in the confidence-estimation literature [20], [21], [29], and has been shown reliable against human annotators [29]. 

Confidence estimators and metrics. The seven estimators group into three inference-only methods that treat the LVLM as a black box (P(True), Self-Probing, Prompt Ensemble) and four trained probes that read or perturb internal representa-tions (SAPLMA, CCPS, InternalInspector, and BICR). Each trained probe follows the architecture reported best in its source paper. Among them, BICR [24] is the only grounding-aware method: it trains its probe with a contrastive objective that penalizes confidence carried over when the image is blacked out, so its score reflects whether the answer used the image. We report calibration with Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and discrimination with AUROC; the selective-prediction quantities (AURC, safe yield, top-decile error) are defined in Section V. Trained probes are trained on GQA over five seeds {23 , 42 , 137 , 2024 , 3407 } with confidence averaged across seeds. IV. R ESULTS ACROSS THREE MEDICAL DOMAINS

The trained probes earn their calibration, and two of them earn it well. Across all three datasets the inference-only baselines discriminate competitively but remain substantially overconfident, with ECE between 0.31 and 0.47. Two trained probes reduce this error dramatically: BICR achieves ECE 0.11 on GMAI and 0.14 on PATH-VQA, while InternalIn-spector reaches 0.03 on SLAKE, the best calibration observed on any medical dataset. Not all trained probes share this benefit, however. CCPS remains overconfident and near chance throughout, indicating that the gains are specific to BICR and InternalInspector rather than trained probes in general. This distinction is clinically important because strong ranking performance alone is insufficient when confidence estimates are used for decision-making. 

Neither standard metric on its own identifies a usable estimator. Discrimination and calibration each fall short when read alone. First, global discrimination is close: on GMAI the best baseline P(True) reaches AUROC 61.9 against 62.9 for the best probe, a gap small enough that ranking alone would call the methods interchangeable. Second, average calibration is cheaply repaired without buying anything, since an off-domain temperature fit on GQA validation drops P(True) GMAI ECE from 0.32 to the level of the trained probes, yet because the fit only rescales scores monotonically the safe automation it enables is unchanged at essentially zero. Average calibration is therefore necessary but neither sufficient nor expensive. What separates the methods is behavior in the high-confidence region a clinician would act on: P(True) assigns confidence above 0.8 to a wrong answer on 41 to 45 percent of its errors on every domain, against 1 to 4 percent for the grounding-aware probe. The signal a triage policy consumes lives in that tail, and only the well-calibrated trained probes have it. 

No single estimator is best, across domains or across models. The preferred method is unstable in two directions. Across domains, BICR and InternalInspector trade the calibra-tion lead (BICR on GMAI and PATH-VQA, InternalInspector on SLAKE), while on pathology no internal probe leads discrimination at all and a prompt ensemble edges ahead on AUROC. Aggregate calibration can hide where a method fails. 

GMAI’s clinical metadata lets us ask whether a method’s calibration is uniform across the imaging landscape, by com-paring the micro aggregate pooled over all cases against the macro aggregate that weights every clinical category equally. On clinical task, department, and perceptual granularity the two agree closely, but on imaging modality they diverge: BICR is well calibrated on the common modalities that dominate the pool (CT, MRI, endoscopy) yet markedly worse on rarer ones (macro ECE 0.22 against micro 0.11). A single pooled number therefore conceals a fairness gap that becomes a concrete deployment risk when a site’s modality mix differs from the benchmark’s, which is why per-category reporting is part of the result rather than an appendix to it. V. D EPLOYMENT AND SAFE DELEGATION 

Aggregate calibration and discrimination do not, by them-selves, answer the question a clinical site faces: how much of this workload can be automated, and which cases must reach a specialist? We recast confidence estimation as bounded selective prediction [11], automating a case only when its confidence clears a threshold and deferring the rest, and we judge methods by what they enable under a clinical error budget rather than by aggregate ranking. For each (dataset, method) we report the area under the risk-coverage curve (AURC, lower is a better selective predictor) and the safe yield, the largest fraction of cases automatable while holding error among automated cases at or below a tolerance (Table I). Calibration is what makes this possible, because a score is actionable only when a threshold of 0.8 really does correspond to roughly 80 percent correct [12]. 

A capability-to-automatability gradient is the central clin-ical finding. Safe yield tracks base-model competence, which in turn tracks each domain’s distance from the training distri-bution. On SLAKE (radiology and anatomy, base accuracy 60.9%) confidence-based triage is genuinely useful: BICR safely automates 34.6% of cases at a 20% error budget and 12.1% at 10%, the best of any method, with InternalInspector (23.8 and 9.1) and P(True) (29.5 and 10.1) behind. On GMAI-MMBench (broad clinical, 47.2%) the same budgets yield only about 4.5 and 1%, with BICR the only method to automate anything at the 10% budget. On PATH-VQA (pathology, 39.5%) no method safely automates even at 20%. The oracle row of Table I makes the structure precise. The ceiling of a perfect-ranking selective predictor falls with base accuracy, from 76% on SLAKE to 59% on GMAI to 49% on pathology at the 20% budget, and the fraction of that ceiling any real method captures falls faster, from 0.45 on SLAKE to 0.08 on GMAI to essentially zero on pathology. The two failures compound: competence lowers the ceiling and the confidence layer leaves more of the remaining headroom unclaimed, so on pathology both bind at once. Notably the pathology ceiling is not zero (a perfect ranker could clear 49% at the 20% budget), so the realized failure there is not base competence alone but the confidence layer failing to find the low-error subset that competence still permits. 

The gradient replicates within each benchmark, and the signal tracks capability independently of the ceiling. Across-dataset gradient could be an artifact of three differently constructed datasets, so we test it inside one. Within GMAI, per-department BICR safe yield at a 20% budget rises with per-department base accuracy across nineteen departments (Pearson r = 0 .85 , or 0.73 with a single high-accuracy outlier removed; two departments never clear the 30-case automation floor and sit at zero). The same relationship holds inside SLAKE across its seven anatomical regions ( r = 0.93 ), an independent replication on a clinically named axis. Safe yield is upper-bounded by base accuracy through the oracle ceiling, so part of this correlation is mechanical; we therefore also correlate base accuracy with confidence AUROC, which carries no such dependence, and find it positive in both cases (0.74 across GMAI departments, 0.88 across SLAKE regions). The quality of the confidence signal itself, not only the ceiling-bounded yield, is thus lowest exactly where the model is weakest, a caution that compounds rather than offsets low accuracy in the hardest domains. 

The gradient is uneven across clinical workstreams. The gradient is not uniform across clinical workstreams, and the pattern is consequential for where an LVLM can be trusted first. Anatomy and modality questions are the reachable ones: on SLAKE the model is fully automatable on modality identification (base accuracy 83%, safe yield 100%) but not at all on abnormality detection (43%, 0%), trustworthy to report that an image is a CT but not to flag a lesion. The cancer-relevant workstreams sit at the other end. In GMAI the oncology-adjacent departments, medical oncology, hema-tology, and laboratory medicine and pathology, all fall in the low-accuracy half and admit essentially no safe automation at a 20% budget for any method, BICR included. Pathol-ogy is the floor for a legible reason: PATH-VQA accuracy degrades from descriptive questions (roughly 57 to 60% on 

are , does , is ) to localization and identification (16 to 25% on 

what , how , where ), the same capability gradient seen across datasets, expressed inside one. The deployment guidance for those workstreams also runs against intuition. Where the base model is this unreliable, the expensive hidden-state probes lose their tail-safety edge: on PATH-VQA a cheap prompt ensemble takes the best AURC (0.490) and a raw self-report the lowest top-decile error (0.34), against 0.53 and 0.47 for the grounding-aware probe. Nobody is safely automating pathology with any method, but the practical lesson is that on the hardest tissue domains the probe machinery is not worth its cost, and a site is better served asking the model more cheaply than instrumenting its internals. 

Where the model is competent, the estimator still matters. 

The verdict is not only domain-specific but model-specific, and because aggregating across models can create or erase a usable operating point, deployability must be read per model rather than from a pooled number. Within that caveat, the choice of estimator earns its place wherever the base model clears a competence floor: BICR is the best selective predictor by AURC on GMAI (0.409) and SLAKE (0.243), and the edge is the high-confidence tail rather than global ranking, since AURC and the confident-error rate reward the same low-error region, where BICR posts the lowest top-decile error of any method (0.25 on GMAI, 0.09 on SLAKE). This reconciles with the per-cell non-separability of AUROC: the advantage is tail-safety, not a uniformly better ordering. 

A grounding-aware score lowers confidence when the im-age is not used. The case a clinician most needs flagged is the answer the model produced without looking at the scan, and only a method built to notice this will catch it. Two pieces of evidence show a grounding-aware score behaves accordingly. On a behavioral test that isolates the image-invariant subpop-ulation of GMAI, keeping the cases whose first generated token is unchanged when the real image is replaced by a random one, the grounding-aware ranking objective is the only one that meaningfully lowers its mean confidence on that subpopulation, roughly halving the calibration error of every baseline there [24]. And on SLAKE’s native question typing, where knowledge questions are answerable largely without the image, the grounding-aware and static probes lower their confidence on knowledge relative to vision questions (BICR by 7.4 points, InternalInspector by 6.5) while the raw self-report stays high and nearly flat (P(True) by 2.2). A score that tracks whether the model used the scan is therefore a designed property, not one that emerges from self-report, and it matters most on the high-stakes answers that ignore the image. 

A sober deployment message. Selective deferral, not raw accuracy, is what allows safe clinical deployment [11]. The honest near-term opportunity is calibrated triage and workload prioritization, and the honest risk is that scores which are merely discriminative, or merely low, are mistaken for safety. As medical LVLMs improve, the safe-yield frontier will rise unevenly, fastest in the models and domains closest to general training data and slowest in pathology, and a well-calibrated, grounding-aware estimator is the signal whose meaning a clinician can rely on along that frontier. VI. D ISCUSSION 

How confidence is measured and aggregated matters more than which model produces it. The deployment verdict is domain- and model-specific and has two levels. Base-model competence sets a ceiling on how much can be automated, and that ceiling collapses from radiology through broad clinical imaging to pathology; the confidence layer then decides how much of the ceiling is safely reachable, and where a model clears a competence floor a calibrated, grounding-aware score is the lever that converts an unreliable LVLM into a triage tool clearing roughly a third of the workload at a moderate budget. On pathology the two failures compound, since competence lowers the ceiling and no method captures the headroom a per-fect ranker still permits, and there the cheap prompt methods are as trustworthy in the tail as the instrumented probes, so the probe machinery is not worth its cost. Both levels are invisible to a pooled leaderboard: the preferred estimator shifts across domain and model, and aggregate calibration conceals where a method fails, as when a probe excellent pooled degrades on rarer imaging modalities, a fairness gap that matters precisely when a deploying site’s case mix differs from the benchmark’s. The portable lesson is that estimators for medical LVLMs must be read per model and per clinical category, and the property a triage policy most needs, a low rate of confident errors in a trustworthy high-confidence region, is the one the standard ranking metric does not measure. The near-term role is calibrated triage with a clinician on every escalated case. 

Limitations. Correctness labels come from a single 

gpt-5-mini judge applied uniformly; the uniform rule removes cross-dataset grading confounds and the protocol is established in prior work, but the judge is not separately validated on these medical sets, where subtle semantic equiv-alence is exactly the hard case, so expert adjudication of a labeled subset is the clear next step. The safe-yield thresholds are operating points whose stability we report but which a deploying site must re-validate against its own case mix and harm profile, and the 10 to 20 percent error budgets are first approximations, since the appropriate tolerance depends on task-specific risk and institutional governance. The probes are evaluated strictly out-of-distribution, trained on natural images, so in-domain medical fine-tuning of the confidence layer is a natural and unstudied extension. Finally, all three datasets are VQA over still images, and absolute discrimination is modest for every method (AUROC near 0.63 even for the best), so the clearest advantage is calibration and safe, bounded-error triage, the property clinical deployment most requires. VII. C ONCLUSION 

Across three medical imaging domains, how confidence is measured and aggregated matters more than which model produces the answer, and it governs how much work can be safely delegated. Safe automation is bounded first by base-model competence, which sets a ceiling that collapses from radiology to pathology, and second by the confidence layer, which decides how much of that ceiling is reachable. The estimators that succeed are those with a trustworthy high-confidence region. Well-calibrated trained probes hold confident-error rates to single digits where baselines exceed 40 percent, and a grounding-aware probe is alone in lowering its confidence when the model answers without using the image, the failure a clinician most needs flagged. Since no method wins across domains or models, sites should validate the confidence layer against their own case mix rather than trust pooled benchmarks. As general medical LVLMs improve, the safe-yield frontier will likely rise, fastest in the domains closest to general training data. As such, a calibrated, grounding-aware estimator is the signal a clinician can rely on, and the role these systems can fill today is calibrated triage, with a clinician on every escalated case.
